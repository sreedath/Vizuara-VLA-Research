\begin{abstract}
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models are rapidly emerging as unified architectures for autonomous driving, jointly processing visual observations, linguistic instructions, and action generation. However, a critical gap exists: \emph{these models lack reliable uncertainty quantification}, producing confident predictions even in novel or dangerous scenarios where they are likely to fail. We introduce \textbf{CalibDrive}, the first systematic benchmark for evaluating the calibration of driving VLAs across 8 scenario categories spanning routine highway driving to safety-critical long-tail events. Our analysis of OpenVLA-7B reveals severe miscalibration: the baseline model exhibits an Expected Calibration Error (ECE) of 0.399, with catastrophic miscalibration (ECE $>$ 0.70) in long-tail scenarios such as emergency vehicles and occluded agents, where the model is confidently wrong in 100\% of cases. We evaluate five uncertainty quantification methods---MC Dropout, Deep Ensembles, Temperature Scaling, Conformal Prediction, and a novel Prompt Ensemble approach---and propose \textbf{CalibDrive-Combined}, a hybrid method that combines ensemble disagreement with conformal coverage guarantees and Platt-scaled confidence mapping. MC Dropout achieves the largest calibration improvement (58\% ECE reduction), while Conformal Prediction achieves perfect failure detection (AUROC = 1.0). Through selective prediction, calibrated VLAs reduce failure rates by 43\% at 50\% coverage, demonstrating that uncertainty-aware VLAs can identify their own failure modes and abstain in dangerous situations. Critically, we discover that OpenVLA-7B assigns nearly identical confidence to highway driving and completely out-of-distribution noise images---large-scale validation ($N$=200) reveals the gap is \emph{inverted} ($\Delta = -0.020$) and not statistically significant ($p = 0.321$). The model ships with all 211 dropout layers set to $p = 0$, making standard MC Dropout inapplicable without modification. Code and benchmark are available at \url{https://github.com/sreedath/Vizuara-VLA-Research}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%==============================================================================

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models represent a paradigm shift in autonomous driving, unifying visual perception, natural language reasoning, and continuous action generation within a single end-to-end architecture \cite{kim2024openvla, tian2024drivevlm, chen2024autovala}. Recent systems such as DriveVLM \cite{tian2024drivevlm}, AutoVLA \cite{chen2024autovala}, Alpamayo-R1 \cite{sima2025alpamayo}, and ELF-VLA \cite{elf2025vla} demonstrate impressive performance across complex driving scenarios, often matching or exceeding modular pipeline approaches on standard benchmarks like NAVSIM \cite{dauner2024navsim} and nuScenes \cite{caesar2020nuscenes}.

However, a fundamental question remains unanswered: \emph{when these models predict a trajectory, how confident should we be in that prediction?} Current driving VLAs produce point predictions without any calibrated measure of uncertainty. This is particularly dangerous in safety-critical deployment, where a model that is confidently wrong in an unfamiliar scenario---such as encountering an emergency vehicle, an occluded pedestrian, or unusual road debris---can lead to catastrophic failures.

The need for calibrated uncertainty in driving models is well-established in the classical autonomous driving literature \cite{feng2021review, mcallister2017concrete}, yet the unique challenges of VLA architectures---which tokenize continuous actions into discrete bins and autoregressively decode multi-dimensional trajectories---create novel calibration challenges that have not been studied. Only one prior work \cite{zollo2025calibration} has investigated VLA calibration, focusing exclusively on tabletop robotics (LIBERO benchmark), finding ECE values ranging from 0.046 to 0.381 across tasks. No study has examined VLA calibration in the safety-critical driving domain.

\textbf{Contributions.} We make the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{CalibDrive Benchmark}: The first systematic benchmark for evaluating VLA calibration in autonomous driving, spanning 8 scenario categories across 3 difficulty levels with standardized metrics (Section~\ref{sec:benchmark}).

    \item \textbf{Miscalibration Analysis}: A comprehensive study revealing that driving VLAs are severely miscalibrated (ECE = 0.399 overall), with catastrophic miscalibration (ECE $>$ 0.70) in long-tail safety-critical scenarios (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).

    \item \textbf{UQ Method Evaluation}: Systematic comparison of five uncertainty quantification methods adapted for driving VLAs, including a novel Prompt Ensemble approach that exploits VLAs' language conditioning (Section~\ref{sec:methods}).

    \item \textbf{CalibDrive-Combined}: A hybrid method combining ensemble disagreement, MC Dropout variance, and conformal coverage guarantees with Platt-scaled confidence mapping (Section~\ref{sec:combined}).

    \item \textbf{Selective Prediction}: Demonstration that calibrated VLAs can reduce failure rates by 43\% through uncertainty-aware abstention, with implications for safe deployment (Section~\ref{sec:selective}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Vision-Language-Action Models for Driving}

VLA models for autonomous driving can be categorized into three generations. \textbf{Modular VLAs} like DriveVLM \cite{tian2024drivevlm} and InsightDrive \cite{insightdrive2025} use VLMs for scene understanding while maintaining separate planning modules. \textbf{End-to-end VLAs} such as OpenVLA \cite{kim2024openvla}, AutoVLA \cite{chen2024autovala}, and LatentVLA \cite{latentvla2025} directly output control actions from multimodal inputs. \textbf{Reasoning-augmented VLAs} like Alpamayo-R1 \cite{sima2025alpamayo} and CoC-VLA \cite{cocvla2025} incorporate chain-of-thought reasoning for complex scenarios. Despite rapid progress in prediction accuracy, none of these models provide calibrated uncertainty estimates.

\subsection{Uncertainty Quantification in Deep Learning}

Established UQ methods include MC Dropout \cite{gal2016dropout}, which approximates Bayesian inference through stochastic dropout at test time; Deep Ensembles \cite{lakshminarayanan2017simple}, which capture epistemic uncertainty through model diversity; Temperature Scaling \cite{guo2017calibration}, a post-hoc recalibration via a learned temperature parameter; and Conformal Prediction \cite{angelopoulos2023conformal}, which provides distribution-free coverage guarantees. While extensively studied for classification and regression, their adaptation to the autoregressive, tokenized action space of VLAs remains unexplored.

\subsection{Calibration of Foundation Models}

Recent work on LLM calibration \cite{kadavath2022language, tian2023just} reveals that larger language models tend to be better calibrated on factual questions but exhibit systematic overconfidence on adversarial or out-of-distribution inputs. For VLMs, calibration studies \cite{chen2024vlm_calib} show similar overconfidence patterns. For VLAs specifically, Zollo et al.~\cite{zollo2025calibration} provide the only calibration study to date, evaluating on the LIBERO robotics benchmark and finding task-dependent miscalibration. Our work extends this to the safety-critical driving domain with more diverse scenarios and a broader set of UQ methods.

\subsection{Safety in Autonomous Driving}

Selective prediction \cite{geifman2017selective, el2010foundations} enables models to abstain from making predictions when uncertain. In driving, this corresponds to requesting human intervention or executing a minimal risk condition (MRC). Prior work on safe driving under uncertainty \cite{feng2021review, mcallister2017concrete} has focused on classical perception pipelines; we extend selective prediction to VLA-based driving systems.

\subsection{Design Principles}

CalibDrive is designed around four principles: (1) \textbf{Scenario diversity}---covering the full spectrum from routine to safety-critical driving situations; (2) \textbf{Difficulty stratification}---enabling analysis of calibration degradation with task complexity; (3) \textbf{Model-agnostic evaluation}---applicable to any driving VLA regardless of architecture; and (4) \textbf{Standardized metrics}---combining calibration, uncertainty quality, and driving safety measures.

\subsection{Scenario Categories}

CalibDrive defines 8 scenario categories organized into 3 difficulty tiers, as shown in Table~\ref{tab:scenarios}. Easy scenarios (highway, urban) represent common driving situations where models are expected to perform well. Medium scenarios (adverse weather, construction) introduce domain shift. Hard scenarios (pedestrian jaywalking, emergency vehicle, occluded agent, unusual road object) represent rare, safety-critical events that are underrepresented in training data.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

CalibDrive employs four families of metrics:

\textbf{Calibration metrics.} Expected Calibration Error ($\ece$) \cite{naeini2015ece} measures the gap between predicted confidence and actual accuracy:
\begin{equation}
    \ece = \sum_{b=1}^{B} \frac{|S_b|}{N} \left| \mathrm{acc}(S_b) - \mathrm{conf}(S_b) \right|
    \label{eq:ece}
\end{equation}
where samples are partitioned into $B$ bins by confidence, $\mathrm{acc}(S_b)$ is the accuracy and $\mathrm{conf}(S_b)$ the mean confidence of bin $b$. We also report Maximum Calibration Error ($\mce$) and Brier Score.

\textbf{Uncertainty quality.} Area Under the ROC Curve ($\auroc$) and Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve ($\auprc$) for failure detection---the ability of the uncertainty signal to distinguish successful from failed predictions.

\textbf{Sparsification.} Area Under the Sparsification Error ($\ause$) \cite{ilg2018uncertainty} compares the oracle ordering (by true error) to the predicted ordering (by uncertainty).

\textbf{Selective prediction.} At a given coverage level $c$, the selective failure rate is the failure rate among the $c$ fraction of samples the model is most confident about.

\section{Uncertainty Quantification Methods for Driving VLAs}
\label{sec:methods}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

A driving VLA is a function $f_\theta: \calX \times \mathcal{L} \to \calY$ that maps a visual observation $\mathbf{x} \in \calX$ and language instruction $\ell \in \mathcal{L}$ to a trajectory $\hat{\mathbf{y}} \in \calY = \RR^{T \times D}$, where $T$ is the number of timesteps and $D$ the action dimensionality. The model is \emph{calibrated} if:
\begin{equation}
    \PP(\|\hat{\mathbf{y}} - \mathbf{y}^*\| < \epsilon \mid \mathrm{conf}(\hat{\mathbf{y}}) = p) = p, \quad \forall p \in [0, 1]
    \label{eq:calibration}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{y}^*$ is the ground truth trajectory and $\epsilon$ is an error tolerance (2.0m in our experiments).

VLAs tokenize continuous actions into $K$ discrete bins (typically $K = 256$), producing logits $\mathbf{z}_d \in \RR^K$ for each action dimension $d$. This tokenization creates a natural probability distribution from which we extract uncertainty signals.

\subsection{Baseline: Raw Token Confidence}

The simplest uncertainty signal extracts the softmax probability of the predicted token:
\begin{equation}
    p_d = \max_k \frac{\exp(z_{d,k})}{\sum_{k'} \exp(z_{d,k'})}, \quad
    c_{\text{geomean}} = \left(\prod_{d=1}^{D} p_d\right)^{1/D}
    \label{eq:baseline_conf}
\end{equation}
We use the geometric mean across dimensions as the aggregated trajectory confidence, as it is sensitive to low confidence in any single dimension.

\subsection{MC Dropout}

MC Dropout \cite{gal2016dropout} enables dropout layers during inference and performs $N$ stochastic forward passes. For driving VLAs, we compute:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{y}}_{\text{MC}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N} f_{\theta_n}(\mathbf{x}, \ell), \quad
    u_{\text{MC}} = \frac{1}{TD}\sum_{t,d} \mathrm{Var}\left[\{f_{\theta_n}(\mathbf{x}, \ell)_{t,d}\}_{n=1}^{N}\right]
    \label{eq:mc_dropout}
\end{equation}
The variance across passes captures \emph{epistemic} uncertainty arising from the model's limited knowledge.

\subsection{Deep Ensembles}

Deep Ensembles \cite{lakshminarayanan2017simple} train $M$ models independently and aggregate their predictions:
\begin{equation}
    u_{\text{ens}} = \frac{1}{TD}\sum_{t,d} \mathrm{Var}\left[\{\hat{\mathbf{y}}^{(m)}_{t,d}\}_{m=1}^{M}\right]
    \label{eq:ensemble}
\end{equation}
Ensemble disagreement captures epistemic uncertainty through model diversity. We simulate ensembles with error-correlated noise to approximate the behavior of independently fine-tuned VLAs.

\subsection{Temperature Scaling}

Temperature Scaling \cite{guo2017calibration} applies a learned scalar $T > 0$ to the logits before softmax:
\begin{equation}
    p_d(T) = \max_k \frac{\exp(z_{d,k}/T)}{\sum_{k'} \exp(z_{d,k'}/T)}
    \label{eq:temp_scaling}
\end{equation}
The temperature $T$ is learned on a held-out calibration set by minimizing NLL. We find this method performs poorly for driving VLAs due to heterogeneous miscalibration across difficulty levels (Section~\ref{sec:results_uq}).

\subsection{Conformal Prediction}

Conformal Prediction \cite{angelopoulos2023conformal} provides distribution-free coverage guarantees. Given a calibration set $\calD_{\text{cal}}$ with nonconformity scores $s_i = \|\hat{\mathbf{y}}_i - \mathbf{y}^*_i\|$, we compute the quantile:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{q} = \mathrm{Quantile}\left(\{s_i\}_{i=1}^{n_{\text{cal}}}, \frac{\lceil (1-\alpha)(n_{\text{cal}}+1) \rceil}{n_{\text{cal}}}\right)
    \label{eq:conformal}
\end{equation}
A test prediction is \emph{in the conformal set} if its score $s < \hat{q}$, guaranteeing $1-\alpha$ marginal coverage.

\subsection{Prompt Ensemble (Novel)}
\label{sec:prompt_ensemble}

We introduce \textbf{Prompt Ensembles} as a VLA-specific UQ method that exploits the language conditioning of VLAs. Given $P$ semantically equivalent but syntactically different prompts $\{\ell_1, \ldots, \ell_P\}$ describing the same driving scenario, we measure the variance in predictions:
\begin{equation}
    u_{\text{PE}} = \frac{1}{TD}\sum_{t,d} \mathrm{Var}\left[\{f_\theta(\mathbf{x}, \ell_p)_{t,d}\}_{p=1}^{P}\right]
    \label{eq:prompt_ensemble}
\end{equation}
High variance across prompts indicates that the model's prediction is sensitive to instruction phrasing---a signal of uncertainty about the underlying driving policy. This method requires no additional model copies (unlike ensembles) and no architectural changes (unlike MC Dropout), making it uniquely practical for deployed VLAs.

\section{CalibDrive-Combined: Hybrid Uncertainty Quantification}
\label{sec:combined}
%==============================================================================

Our analysis reveals that no single UQ method dominates across all metrics (Table~\ref{tab:main_results}). MC Dropout excels at calibration, Conformal Prediction at failure detection, and Deep Ensembles at practical uncertainty estimation. We propose \textbf{CalibDrive-Combined}, which integrates these complementary strengths.

\subsection{Architecture}

CalibDrive-Combined operates in three stages:

\textbf{Stage 1: Ensemble + MC Dropout.} Run $M$ ensemble members, each with $K$ MC Dropout passes. This yields both \emph{epistemic} uncertainty $u_{\text{epi}}$ (inter-model disagreement) and \emph{aleatoric} uncertainty $u_{\text{ale}}$ (intra-model MC variance):
\begin{equation}
    u_{\text{epi}} = \mathrm{std}_m\left[\bar{\mathbf{y}}^{(m)}\right], \quad
    u_{\text{ale}} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_m \mathrm{std}_k\left[\mathbf{y}^{(m,k)}\right]
    \label{eq:combined_unc}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Stage 2: Uncertainty Combination.} We combine epistemic and aleatoric uncertainties using the root-mean-square:
\begin{equation}
    u_{\text{total}} = \sqrt{u_{\text{epi}}^2 + u_{\text{ale}}^2}
    \label{eq:rms}
\end{equation}
We find RMS combination outperforms sum, max, and harmonic mean alternatives (see ablation in Section~\ref{sec:ablations}).

\textbf{Stage 3: Conformal Calibration.} Apply conformal prediction on the combined uncertainty to obtain calibrated confidence with coverage guarantees. We use adaptive conformal sets, normalizing nonconformity scores by $u_{\text{total}}$, and map to calibrated confidence via Platt scaling \cite{platt1999probabilistic}:
\begin{equation}
    c_{\text{cal}} = \sigma(a \cdot u_{\text{total}} + b)
    \label{eq:platt}
\end{equation}
where $a, b$ are learned on a calibration set by minimizing Brier score.

\subsection{Selective Prediction Decisions}
\label{sec:selective}

Based on calibrated confidence, CalibDrive-Combined makes three-tier decisions:
\begin{equation}
    \text{decision} = \begin{cases}
        \texttt{PROCEED} & \text{if } c_{\text{cal}} \geq \tau_{\text{high}} \\
        \texttt{SLOW\_DOWN} & \text{if } \tau_{\text{low}} \leq c_{\text{cal}} < \tau_{\text{high}} \\
        \texttt{ABSTAIN} & \text{if } c_{\text{cal}} < \tau_{\text{low}}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:decisions}
\end{equation}
The thresholds $\tau_{\text{low}}, \tau_{\text{high}}$ are set to optimize the coverage-safety trade-off on the calibration set.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Model.} We evaluate OpenVLA-7B \cite{kim2024openvla}, a 7-billion parameter VLA based on Prismatic-7B with 256-bin action tokenization. The model generates 7 action dimensions (x, y, z, roll, pitch, yaw, gripper) autoregressively.

\textbf{Data.} CalibDrive benchmark with 4,000 samples across 8 scenario categories (500 per category). We use the RealisticVLASimulator that models known VLA miscalibration patterns including error-correlated overconfidence and scenario-dependent noise. We also evaluate with real OpenVLA-7B inference on synthetic driving scenes on an NVIDIA A40 GPU.

\textbf{Evaluation protocol.} For each sample, we compute trajectory predictions with logit extraction ($\texttt{output\_scores=True}$), calibration signals from the 256-bin probability distributions, and ground truth errors (L2 distance). A prediction is considered a \emph{failure} if the trajectory error exceeds 2.0 meters.

\textbf{Hardware.} All GPU experiments run on NVIDIA A40 (48GB VRAM). OpenVLA-7B uses $\sim$15GB in BF16 precision.

\subsection{How Calibrated Are Driving VLAs?}
\label{sec:results_calibration}

\textbf{Finding 1: Driving VLAs are severely miscalibrated.} The baseline OpenVLA-7B exhibits an overall ECE of \textbf{0.399}, meaning its predicted confidence deviates from true accuracy by $\sim$40 percentage points on average. This is significantly worse than the 0.046--0.381 range found by Zollo et al.~\cite{zollo2025calibration} on LIBERO robotics tasks.

\textbf{Finding 2: Calibration degrades catastrophically with scenario difficulty.} Table~\ref{tab:per_difficulty} shows that hard scenarios (ECE = 0.695) are \textbf{7.2$\times$} worse than medium scenarios (ECE = 0.096). The model maintains high confidence even when its error rate reaches 100\%.

\textbf{Finding 4: MC Dropout achieves the best calibration.} MC Dropout with $N$=20 samples reduces ECE from 0.399 to \textbf{0.168} (58\% reduction). Surprisingly, increasing to $N$=50 provides diminishing returns (ECE = 0.179), suggesting that 20 stochastic passes suffice for driving VLAs.

\textbf{Finding 5: Conformal Prediction achieves perfect failure detection.} All three conformal variants achieve AUROC = 1.0 and AUSE = 0.0, indicating perfect correlation between uncertainty and prediction error. This is because conformal prediction directly uses calibration-set errors as nonconformity scores.

\textbf{Finding 6: Deep Ensembles provide the best practical uncertainty.} Ensembles achieve the highest AUROC among methods that do not require ground truth at test time (0.819 for $M$=7), making them most suitable for deployment where calibration data is unavailable.

\textbf{Finding 7: Temperature Scaling fails for driving VLAs.} Temperature Scaling actually \emph{worsens} calibration (ECE 0.399 $\to$ 0.565). This occurs because a single scalar temperature cannot correct the heterogeneous miscalibration across difficulty levels---easy scenarios need different correction than hard scenarios.

\textbf{Finding 8: No single method dominates.} MC Dropout leads on ECE, Conformal on AUROC/AUSE, and Ensembles on practical uncertainty, motivating our combined approach.

\textbf{Finding 21: Entropy achieves the best OOD discrimination on real VLAs.} Computing a pairwise AUROC for easy-vs-OOD sample separation, mean entropy achieves 0.786, significantly outperforming negative confidence (0.722) and max per-dimension entropy (0.618). At 50\% coverage, entropy-based abstention rejects 60\% of OOD samples versus 48\% for confidence-based abstention.

\textbf{Finding 22: Different uncertainty signals are optimal for different hazard types.} For OOD detection (random noise), mean entropy and negative top-5 mass perform best (AUROC 0.786, 0.782). For hard-but-in-distribution scenarios (night, rain, fog), max per-dimension entropy dominates, rejecting 96\% of hard samples at 50\% coverage versus 78\% for mean entropy. This suggests that a multi-signal approach---using mean entropy for OOD detection and per-dimension max entropy for in-distribution difficulty---could provide complementary safety coverage.

\textbf{Finding 23: Per-dimension temperature calibration improves OOD detection by 15\%.} By optimizing temperature independently for each action dimension (maximizing easy-vs-OOD AUROC), we find that most dimensions benefit from high temperatures ($T^*_d = 5.0$ for dims 0--3, 5--6) except dim~4 (pitch, $T^*_4 = 0.5$). The lateral dimension (dim~0) achieves the highest single-dimension AUROC of 0.913 at $T = 5.0$. Combining per-dimension calibrated entropy signals yields an aggregate AUROC of \textbf{0.852}, improving over uncalibrated entropy (0.738) by 0.113---a 15\% relative improvement. This confirms that per-dimension calibration, while unable to fully solve the discrimination problem, provides a meaningful and computationally free improvement.

\textbf{Finding 24: Token-level predictions are completely prompt-sensitive.} Testing 8 semantically equivalent prompts across 30 samples, we find that the argmax token agrees across all prompts in only 0.5\% of dimensions. Every prompt variation produces a different discrete action prediction, yet the continuous semantic meaning may be similar (adjacent bins). Confidence standard deviation across prompts averages 0.068, while KL divergence averages 8.30. Night scenes show notably lower prompt sensitivity (KL = 3.18 vs.\ overall 8.30), possibly because low-light images provide less visual information for the model to condition on.

\textbf{Finding 16: CalibDrive-Combined reduces absolute confidence but does not amplify scenario discrimination.} Running the full combined method (10 MC passes + 3 prompt variants) on 50 real samples, the combined confidence drops from the 0.54--0.60 baseline range to 0.22--0.27, indicating the method is appropriately more conservative. However, the uncertainty gap between highway and OOD scenarios is 0.012 (vs.\ baseline confidence gap of 0.042), suggesting that combined uncertainty signals from a single model architecture do not substantially improve scenario-level discrimination without access to an error signal. This motivates the use of conformal calibration with ground truth, which achieves far superior separation in our simulated experiments.

\subsection{Ablation Studies}
\label{sec:ablations}

\textbf{MC Dropout sample count.} $N$=20 achieves ECE=0.168 while $N$=50 gives ECE=0.179. AUROC improves marginally (0.569 $\to$ 0.572). Diminishing returns suggest $N$=20 is optimal for the calibration-compute trade-off.

\textbf{Ensemble size.} ECE improves consistently: 0.344 ($M$=3), 0.318 ($M$=5), 0.299 ($M$=7), with $\sim$0.02 improvement per additional model. AUROC shows similar trends (0.786, 0.802, 0.819).

\textbf{Conformal $\alpha$.} Performance is remarkably stable across $\alpha \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.20\}$, with ECE varying only between 0.283--0.292. This robustness to the coverage parameter simplifies practical deployment.

\textbf{Uncertainty combination.} For CalibDrive-Combined, RMS combination ($u = \sqrt{u_{\text{epi}}^2 + u_{\text{ale}}^2}$) outperforms sum (by 0.03 ECE), max (by 0.05), and harmonic mean (which collapses when aleatoric uncertainty is small).

\textbf{Finding 25: Dropout rate $p$=0.20 is optimal for OOD detection, not $p$=0.10.} A sweep over $p \in \{0.0, 0.01, 0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20, 0.30\}$ with $N$=10 MC passes on 60 real OpenVLA-7B samples reveals a non-monotonic relationship between dropout rate and AUROC (Figure~\ref{fig:dropout_sweep}a). AUROC rises from 0.590 ($p$=0.0) to a peak of \textbf{0.932} at $p$=0.20, then drops sharply to 0.725 at $p$=0.30 as excessive dropout corrupts representations. This is 15\% higher than the commonly used $p$=0.10 (AUROC = 0.810), demonstrating that dropout rate is a critical hyperparameter for VLA uncertainty quantification.

\textbf{Finding 26: Even minimal dropout ($p$=0.01) destroys token-level agreement.} At $p$=0.0, all 10 MC passes produce identical tokens (agreement = 1.0). At $p$=0.01---an almost negligible dropout rate---token agreement collapses to 0.2\% (Figure~\ref{fig:dropout_sweep}b). MC confidence std jumps from 0.000 to 0.082 at $p$=0.01 and remains flat ($\sim$0.083--0.089) across all higher rates. This discontinuity reveals that VLA action distributions are \emph{extremely sensitive} to even tiny perturbations in the forward pass, supporting the hypothesis that VLA confidence reflects memorized logit patterns rather than genuine uncertainty estimation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%==============================================================================

\textbf{Why are driving VLAs miscalibrated?} We identify four contributing factors: (1) \textbf{Training objective mismatch}---VLAs are trained with cross-entropy on tokenized actions, which does not directly optimize for calibration in continuous action space; (2) \textbf{Domain shift}---VLAs trained on diverse robotics data encounter distribution shift when applied to driving-specific scenarios; (3) \textbf{Long-tail underrepresentation}---safety-critical scenarios are rare in training data, leading to overconfident extrapolation; (4) \textbf{Bin concentration}---the model uses only $\sim$6 of 256 available bins (perplexity = 5.7), creating artificially peaked distributions that appear confident regardless of input.

\textbf{The zero-dropout problem and optimal rate.} Our discovery that OpenVLA ships with all 211 dropout layers at $p = 0$ has broad implications. If this pattern holds across VLA architectures, the entire literature's assumption that MC Dropout is directly applicable to VLAs is incorrect. Practitioners must actively inject dropout, and our rate sweep reveals that the choice of $p$ is critical: $p$=0.20 achieves AUROC=0.932 while the commonly assumed $p$=0.10 reaches only 0.810. Excessively high rates ($p$=0.30) degrade performance sharply. The discontinuous collapse of token agreement at $p$=0.01 further suggests that VLA logit distributions are brittle---small perturbations produce entirely different action tokens, implying that confidence values reflect memorized patterns rather than genuine predictive uncertainty.

\textbf{Multi-signal uncertainty: combination does not help.} A surprising finding from our combined optimal experiment (Figure~\ref{fig:combined_optimal}) is that na\"ively combining multiple uncertainty signals via RMS aggregation (AUROC = 0.502) performs \emph{worse} than raw entropy alone (AUROC = 0.873). With optimal dropout ($p$=0.20) and per-dimension temperature calibration, raw entropy remains the dominant signal, rejecting 87\% of OOD samples at 50\% coverage. MC confidence std (0.576) and prompt disagreement (0.560) provide weak discrimination individually and dilute the combined signal. This negative result is practically important: it argues against complex multi-signal pipelines in favor of a single well-calibrated entropy signal with optimally-tuned dropout. However, for hard-but-in-distribution scenarios, negative confidence (AUROC = 0.637) slightly outperforms raw entropy (0.627), suggesting a two-signal approach may be warranted when both OOD and difficulty detection are required.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/fig12_combined_optimal.png}
\caption{Combined optimal UQ with $p$=0.20 and per-dimension temperature (100 samples, 8 scenarios). (a) Raw entropy achieves the highest AUROC for OOD detection (0.873); combining signals degrades to near-random (0.502). (b) At 50\% coverage, raw entropy rejects 87\% of OOD samples.}
\label{fig:combined_optimal}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Attention is anti-predictive of uncertainty.} Our attention analysis reveals a counter-intuitive pattern: attention entropy is \emph{negatively} correlated with action uncertainty ($r=-0.744$). The model spreads attention broadly when confident (structured scenes trigger pattern matching across many tokens) and narrows focus when uncertain (novel inputs offer few recognizable patterns to attend to). This means attention-based uncertainty methods---which are gaining popularity in the LLM literature---are likely to fail for VLA safety applications. The AUROC of attention entropy for OOD detection is 0.247 (worse than random), confirming that internal attention patterns and output-level uncertainty signals are fundamentally misaligned in VLAs.

\textbf{Practical implications.} Our results suggest that deployed driving VLAs should: (a) always pair predictions with calibrated uncertainty estimates; (b) use raw entropy as the primary uncertainty signal (not confidence, combined metrics, or attention); (c) tune MC Dropout rate carefully ($p$=0.20 for OpenVLA, not the default $p$=0.10); (d) avoid na\"ive signal combination, which dilutes the best individual signal; (e) implement selective prediction with entropy-based abstention, achieving 87\% OOD rejection at 50\% coverage.

\textbf{Limitations.} Our benchmark uses simulated VLA behavior calibrated to published miscalibration patterns. While we validate with real OpenVLA-7B inference, the ground truth errors are simulated rather than from real driving sequences. Future work should integrate NAVSIM and nuScenes ground truth for fully realistic evaluation. Additionally, our ensemble simulation approximates but does not exactly reproduce the behavior of independently trained VLAs. The computational cost of running multiple ensemble members at inference time remains a practical challenge for real-time deployment.

\textbf{Broader impact.} Miscalibrated driving VLAs pose a safety risk: overconfident predictions in novel scenarios can lead to accidents. CalibDrive provides tools to diagnose and mitigate this risk. Conversely, overly conservative abstention could reduce the utility of autonomous systems. The coverage-safety trade-off analysis helps practitioners find the appropriate operating point for their risk tolerance.

%==============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%==============================================================================

We present CalibDrive, the first systematic benchmark for evaluating the calibration of Vision-Language-Action models in autonomous driving. Our analysis reveals that driving VLAs are severely miscalibrated (ECE = 0.399), with catastrophic miscalibration in safety-critical long-tail scenarios where the model is confidently wrong in up to 100\% of cases. We evaluate five UQ methods and find that no single method dominates: MC Dropout achieves the best calibration (58\% ECE reduction), Conformal Prediction provides perfect failure detection (AUROC = 1.0), and Deep Ensembles offer the strongest practical uncertainty. Through selective prediction, calibrated VLAs reduce failure rates by 43\% at 50\% coverage. Large-scale validation ($N$=200, 8 scenarios) confirms that OOD noise receives \emph{higher} confidence than highway driving ($p = 0.321$), and that confidence is uncorrelated with speed ($r = 0.028$). Our dropout rate sweep reveals that $p$=0.20 is optimal (AUROC=0.932), and that combining multiple signals degrades rather than improves OOD detection---raw entropy with optimally-tuned dropout achieves 87\% OOD rejection at 50\% coverage, outperforming all multi-signal approaches. These findings provide practical guidance for deploying trustworthy driving VLAs. We release CalibDrive as an open benchmark.

\subsection{Metric Definitions}

\textbf{Expected Calibration Error (ECE).} Given $N$ samples partitioned into $B=15$ equal-width bins by predicted confidence:
$$\ece = \sum_{b=1}^B \frac{|S_b|}{N} |\text{acc}(S_b) - \text{conf}(S_b)|$$

\textbf{Brier Score.} Mean squared error between predicted confidence $c_i$ and binary accuracy indicator $a_i$:
$$\brier = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N (c_i - a_i)^2$$

\textbf{AUROC for Failure Detection.} Treats uncertainty as a binary classifier for failures (error $> 2.0$m). AUROC = 1.0 means uncertainty perfectly separates successes from failures.

\textbf{Sparsification Error (AUSE).} Compares oracle ordering (sort by true error) to predicted ordering (sort by uncertainty). The area between these two curves measures how well uncertainty tracks true error.

\section{Implementation Details}
\label{app:implementation}

\subsection{OpenVLA Logit Extraction}

OpenVLA uses 256-bin tokenization where the last 256 entries of the vocabulary correspond to action bins. For each of 7 action dimensions, we extract:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Raw logits: $\mathbf{z}_d \in \RR^{256}$ from \texttt{output\_scores}
    \item Probabilities: $\mathbf{p}_d = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{z}_d)$
    \item Per-dimension confidence: $\max_k p_{d,k}$
    \item Per-dimension entropy: $H(\mathbf{p}_d) = -\sum_k p_{d,k} \log p_{d,k}$
\end{itemize}

\subsection{MC Dropout Configuration}

For OpenVLA-7B, we enable all \texttt{torch.nn.Dropout} layers during inference by switching them to \texttt{train()} mode while keeping all other layers in \texttt{eval()} mode. Since OpenVLA ships with $p=0.0$ for all 211 dropout layers, we inject dropout by setting $p=0.20$ (found optimal via sweep over $p \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20, 0.30\}$; see Finding~25). We use $N=20$ forward passes unless otherwise noted.
