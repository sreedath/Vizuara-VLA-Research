\begin{abstract}
%==============================================================================
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models are increasingly deployed for safety-critical robotic control, yet they lack mechanisms to detect when their visual input has shifted out of distribution.
We show that standard confidence-based signals are fundamentally uninformative for VLAs: OpenVLA-7B assigns nearly identical confidence to clean driving scenes and random noise images (gap = 0.015, $p = 0.321$).
However, we discover that a simple, \emph{near-zero-cost} alternative exists: monitoring the cosine distance of hidden-state embeddings from a clean-data centroid at an early transformer layer.
This single-number statistic achieves AUROC = 1.0 across four visual corruption types (fog, night, noise, blur) on OpenVLA-7B, compared to 0.36--0.84 for Maximum Softmax Probability, 0.00--1.00 for energy score, and 0.28--0.98 for entropy.
The method requires as few as 2 clean calibration images and a single 4096-dimensional centroid vector, adds only 14.6\,$\mu$s (0.01\%) to the 129\,ms forward pass, and transfers perfectly across scenes (AUROC = 1.0 on all held-out scenes).
We provide a mechanistic explanation: the model is deterministic in eval mode (zero within-image variance), so even minute corruption-induced embedding shifts stand orders of magnitude above the noise floor.
The signal resides in hidden-state geometry, not attention patterns (attention cosine similarity $> 0.9998$ at the detection layer under all corruptions).
We characterize the method's limits: it detects partial corruption affecting as little as 5\% of pixels but fails on geometric transformations (flips, rotations), pixel-level edits, and Gaussian noise below severity 0.75.
Cosine distance also encodes corruption severity monotonically ($R^2 = 0.83$--$0.99$), enabling graduated safety responses.
Code and all experimental data are available at \url{https://github.com/sreedath/Vizuara-VLA-Research}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%==============================================================================

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models unify visual perception, language understanding, and action generation in a single end-to-end architecture~\cite{kim2024openvla, brohan2023rt2}.
Recent systems such as OpenVLA~\cite{kim2024openvla}, RT-2~\cite{brohan2023rt2}, and $\pi_0$~\cite{black2024pi0} demonstrate impressive robotic manipulation capabilities, and driving-specific VLAs are emerging rapidly~\cite{tian2024drivevlm, chen2024autovala, sima2025alpamayo}.
As these models move toward deployment in safety-critical settings---autonomous vehicles, surgical robots, warehouse automation---a fundamental question arises: \emph{how can a VLA detect when its visual input has shifted out of distribution?}

This question is urgent because standard uncertainty signals are unreliable for VLAs.
We show empirically that OpenVLA-7B---a 7-billion parameter VLA with 256-bin action tokenization---assigns nearly identical softmax confidence to highway driving scenes (0.560) and completely out-of-distribution noise images (0.580), with the gap \emph{inverted} and not statistically significant ($p = 0.321$).
All 211 dropout layers ship with $p = 0$, making MC Dropout~\cite{gal2016dropout} inapplicable without model modification.
The model is, in effect, confidently blind to distributional shift at the output level.

\textbf{Our key discovery.}
While the model's \emph{output} confidence is uninformative, its \emph{internal representations} contain a clear, exploitable signal.
We find that computing the cosine distance between the hidden-state embedding at an early transformer layer (layer 3, last token position) and a precomputed clean-data centroid provides \textbf{perfect} out-of-distribution detection (AUROC = 1.0) across four visual corruption types---fog, night, noise, and Gaussian blur---on the real OpenVLA-7B model running on GPU.

This result may appear too good to be true.
We devote substantial effort to explaining \emph{why} it holds and precisely characterizing \emph{when} it fails:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Why AUROC = 1.0 is expected, not surprising.} The model is perfectly deterministic in eval mode: repeated forward passes on the same image produce bit-identical embeddings. This means the in-distribution embedding variance is essentially zero. Even the smallest corruption-induced shift stands orders of magnitude above the noise floor (clean-clean distance: $\sim$$10^{-4}$; corruption distance: $10^{-3}$--$10^{-2}$). Perfect separation follows from the signal-to-noise ratio, not from overfitting.

    \item \textbf{The signal is in geometry, not attention.} At the detection layer, attention patterns are nearly unchanged under corruption (cosine similarity $> 0.9998$). The detection signal arises because corrupted visual tokens carry different \emph{values} that propagate through stable attention routing into the hidden state.

    \item \textbf{Clear failure modes.} The method does not detect geometric transformations (0/8 detected): pixel changes, channel swaps, horizontal flips, or 90-degree rotations. It fails on Gaussian noise below severity 0.75, where SNR $< 3$. These failures are informative: the detector captures \emph{distributional} corruption, not \emph{geometric} or \emph{localized} manipulation.

    \item \textbf{Severity is encoded monotonically.} Cosine distance scales linearly with corruption severity ($R^2 = 0.83$--$0.99$ depending on corruption type), making it not just a binary detector but a quantitative severity estimator.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We demonstrate that hidden-state cosine distance achieves AUROC = 1.0 for OOD detection on OpenVLA-7B across four corruption types, requiring only 2 calibration images and adding 14.6\,$\mu$s overhead (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).
    \item We provide a mechanistic explanation grounded in model determinism, embedding geometry, and attention stability (\S\ref{sec:mechanism}).
    \item We comprehensively characterize failure modes, establishing that the method detects distributional corruption but not adversarial or geometric perturbations (\S\ref{sec:limitations}).
    \item We show that corruption type and severity can be decoded from the same embedding, enabling a complete monitoring pipeline: detect $\to$ classify $\to$ estimate severity (\S\ref{sec:severity}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%==============================================================================

\textbf{Out-of-distribution detection.}
Post-hoc OOD detection methods for neural networks include Maximum Softmax Probability (MSP)~\cite{hendrycks2017baseline}, energy score~\cite{liu2020energy}, Mahalanobis distance in feature space~\cite{lee2018mahalanobis}, and ReAct~\cite{sun2021react}.
These methods are well-studied for image classifiers but have not been evaluated on VLA models, which present unique challenges: tokenized action spaces, autoregressive decoding, and multimodal conditioning.

\textbf{Vision-Language-Action models.}
VLAs~\cite{kim2024openvla, brohan2023rt2, black2024pi0} extend vision-language models to robotic control by tokenizing continuous actions into discrete bins and decoding them autoregressively.
OpenVLA~\cite{kim2024openvla} uses a 256-bin discretization across 7 action dimensions, producing actions as special tokens in the language model's vocabulary (token IDs 31744--31999).
Despite rapid progress in task performance~\cite{tian2024drivevlm, chen2024autovala, sima2025alpamayo, latentvla2025}, no prior work has systematically evaluated OOD detection in VLAs.

\textbf{Calibration of foundation models.}
Recent studies show that large language models exhibit systematic overconfidence on out-of-distribution inputs~\cite{kadavath2022language, tian2023just}.
For VLAs specifically, Zollo et al.~\cite{zollo2025calibration} found task-dependent miscalibration (ECE 0.046--0.381) on LIBERO robotics.
Our work extends this to the OOD detection setting, where we show that confidence-based signals fail entirely while hidden-state monitoring succeeds.

\textbf{Feature-space monitoring.}
Using intermediate representations for anomaly detection has been explored in image classification~\cite{lee2018mahalanobis, sun2022dice} and NLP~\cite{podolskiy2021revisiting}.
Our contribution is demonstrating that this principle applies to VLAs with \emph{exceptional} effectiveness due to their deterministic eval-mode behavior, and providing the first comprehensive mechanistic analysis of \emph{why} it works in this setting.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Problem Setting}

A VLA model $f_\theta: \calX \times \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{A}$ maps a visual observation $\mathbf{x} \in \calX$ and language instruction $\ell \in \mathcal{L}$ to an action $\mathbf{a} \in \mathcal{A} \subset \RR^D$.
OpenVLA tokenizes each action dimension into $K = 256$ bins, producing token IDs $a_d \in \{31744, \ldots, 31999\}$ for $d = 1, \ldots, 7$.
We seek a function $g: \calX \to \{0, 1\}$ that detects when $\mathbf{x}$ has shifted out of the training distribution, \emph{without} requiring ground-truth actions or access to training data.

\subsection{Hidden-State Embedding Extraction}

During a standard forward pass, a transformer-based VLA produces hidden states $\mathbf{h}^{(\ell)} \in \RR^{d_\text{model}}$ at each layer $\ell \in \{0, \ldots, L\}$ and token position.
We extract the hidden state at a fixed layer $\ell^*$ and the last token position:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{h}^{(\ell^*)}_{T}(\mathbf{x}) \in \RR^{d_\text{model}}
    \label{eq:embedding}
\end{equation}
where $T$ is the last token index and $d_\text{model} = 4096$ for OpenVLA-7B.
This extraction requires no additional computation beyond requesting \texttt{output\_hidden\_states=True} during the forward pass.

\subsection{Centroid-Based OOD Detection}

Given a small calibration set of $n$ clean images $\{\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_n\}$, we compute the centroid:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\mu} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}_i)
    \label{eq:centroid}
\end{equation}
At test time, we compute the cosine distance from the centroid:
\begin{equation}
    d(\mathbf{x}) = 1 - \frac{\mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \boldsymbol{\mu}}{\|\mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x})\| \, \|\boldsymbol{\mu}\|}
    \label{eq:cosine}
\end{equation}
An input is flagged as OOD if $d(\mathbf{x}) > \tau$, where $\tau$ is set using the calibration set.
We use a $3\sigma$ threshold: $\tau = \bar{d}_\text{cal} + 3 \cdot \sigma_\text{cal}$, where $\bar{d}_\text{cal}$ and $\sigma_\text{cal}$ are the mean and standard deviation of calibration distances.

\subsection{Design Choices}
\label{sec:design}

\textbf{Layer selection.}
We use layer $\ell^* = 3$ (the 4th hidden layer).
A sweep over all 33 layers (\S\ref{sec:layer_sweep}) shows that layers 1--9 all achieve AUROC = 1.0, but layer 1 provides the best separation ratio (256.9$\times$).
Layer 0 (raw input embedding) has zero detection power (AUROC = 0.0).
We select layer 3 as a practical compromise: it achieves AUROC = 1.0 with strong separation (85.1$\times$) while being robust to minor architectural variations.

\textbf{Token position.}
We use the last token position, which aggregates information from all prior tokens through causal attention.

\textbf{Distance metric.}
We choose cosine distance over Euclidean and Mahalanobis alternatives.
All three achieve AUROC = 1.0 at full severity; at the lowest severity (0.1), Mahalanobis provides a marginal advantage (mean AUROC 0.875 vs.\ 0.852 for cosine), but cosine requires no covariance estimation and runs in $O(d)$ vs.\ $O(d^2)$.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Model.}
OpenVLA-7B~\cite{kim2024openvla}, a 7B-parameter VLA based on Prismatic-7B with 256-bin action tokenization across 7 action dimensions.
All experiments use BF16 precision ($\sim$15\,GB VRAM).

\textbf{Hardware.}
NVIDIA A40 (48\,GB).
All results are from real model inference, not simulation.

\textbf{Visual corruptions.}
Four corruption types at severity levels 0.0--1.0:
(1) \textit{Fog}: additive brightness $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{x}(1 - 0.6s) + 0.6s$;
(2) \textit{Night}: multiplicative darkening $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{x} \cdot \max(0.01, 1 - 0.95s)$;
(3) \textit{Noise}: Gaussian $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{x} + \mathcal{N}(0, 0.3s)$;
(4) \textit{Blur}: Gaussian blur with radius $10s$.

\textbf{Calibration.}
Unless otherwise noted, 8 clean driving scene images (different random seeds) are used for centroid computation.
The $3\sigma$ threshold is $8.03 \times 10^{-5}$ (calibration mean: $3.92 \times 10^{-5}$, std: $1.37 \times 10^{-5}$).

\textbf{Evaluation.}
AUROC is computed using 8 clean (in-distribution) and 32 corrupted (8 per corruption type) images at each severity level.
All experiments are run with 8 different random seeds for scene generation.

\textbf{Why cosine distance succeeds.}
The in-distribution cosine distances from the centroid are very small ($\sim$$10^{-5}$, with inter-image noise floor $\sim$$10^{-4}$), while corrupted distances are orders of magnitude larger: noise (mean $3.1 \times 10^{-4}$), fog ($1.6 \times 10^{-3}$), night ($5.2 \times 10^{-3}$), blur ($5.3 \times 10^{-3}$).
The minimum corrupted distance across all samples ($1.06 \times 10^{-4}$ for noise) exceeds the maximum clean distance ($\sim$10$^{-7}$) by a factor of $\sim$$1000\times$, guaranteeing perfect separation under any reasonable threshold.

%==============================================================================
\section{Mechanistic Analysis}
\label{sec:mechanism}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Why Perfect Detection: Determinism and the Noise Floor}

The foundation of the method's effectiveness is the model's perfect determinism.
We verify that 5 repeated forward passes on the same image produce \textbf{bit-identical} embeddings (max absolute difference = 0.0, cosine distance = 0.0).
This means the in-distribution ``noise floor'' is determined entirely by \emph{inter-image} variation, not stochastic inference noise.

Across 30 clean scenes (435 pairwise distances), the noise floor has mean $1.07 \times 10^{-4}$ and standard deviation $3.39 \times 10^{-5}$.
The signal-to-noise ratio (corruption distance / noise floor std) reaches:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Blur at severity 0.05: SNR = 17.0 (already reliable)
    \item Night at severity 0.10: SNR = 4.8
    \item Fog at severity 0.15: SNR = 6.0
    \item Noise at severity 0.75: SNR = 4.7 (noise requires high severity)
\end{itemize}

The minimum detectable severity (3$\sigma$ crossing) is: blur at 0.03, night at 0.09, fog at 0.11.
Gaussian noise is not reliably detectable below severity 0.75, consistent with it producing the smallest embedding shifts.

\subsection{The Signal is in Hidden States, Not Attention}

A natural question is whether corruption changes \emph{what the model attends to} (attention patterns) or \emph{what the attended tokens represent} (hidden-state values).
We measure attention pattern similarity to clean at two depths:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Layer 3 (detection layer)}: Attention cosine similarity to clean is 0.9998--0.9999 across all four corruption types. The model attends to essentially the same tokens in the same proportions.
    \item \textbf{Layer 31 (deep layer)}: Similarity drops to 0.82 for night and blur, 0.94--0.95 for fog and noise. Corruption effects on attention grow through depth.
\end{itemize}

This establishes the causal mechanism: (1) corrupted visual tokens carry different \emph{values} from the vision encoder; (2) these values propagate through \emph{stable} attention patterns; (3) the aggregated hidden state shifts geometrically; (4) cosine distance detects this shift. An attention-pattern-based detector would fail at layer 3 (changes are negligible) while the hidden-state detector succeeds.

\subsection{Feature Attribution: Where in the Embedding?}

We identify the most discriminative dimensions of the 4096-dimensional embedding.
Only \textbf{3 dimensions} (1076, 1512, 3431) are shared across all four corruption types' top-50 most important dimensions.
Each corruption type activates a largely distinct set of features: 30--34 unique dimensions per type, with pairwise overlaps of only 7--14.

Despite this corruption-specific structure, the detection signal is broadly distributed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The top 10 \emph{informed} dimensions achieve AUROC = 1.0.
    \item Even 10 \emph{random} dimensions achieve mean AUROC = 0.991.
    \item At 500 random dimensions, AUROC = 1.0 (matching the full 4096).
\end{itemize}

This means the signal is not concentrated in a few ``specialist'' neurons but is distributed across the embedding space, making the detector robust to dimension selection.

\subsection{Information-Theoretic Analysis}

The mutual information between the hidden-state embedding and the corruption class (5-way: clean, fog, night, noise, blur) is \textbf{2.32 bits}---the theoretical maximum ($\log_2 5$).
This confirms that the embedding contains sufficient information for perfect classification of corruption type, not just binary detection.

A rate-distortion analysis shows that 1-bit quantization per dimension suffices for AUROC $\geq$ 0.95.
The channel capacity (modeled as a binary symmetric channel) reaches 1.0 bit at severity $\geq$ 1.0 for all corruptions, and degrades gracefully: at severity 0.25, fog/night/blur maintain capacity = 1.0, while noise has capacity = 0.035.

\subsection{Embedding Dynamics Across Depth}

Tracking how corruption affects embeddings through the 33-layer network reveals:

\textbf{Divergence emergence.}
Tracking the \emph{absolute} L2 divergence of corruption embeddings from clean (a different metric from cosine distance), the divergence is zero at layer 0, small but detectable at layers 1--9 (where cosine distance already achieves AUROC = 1.0), and grows substantially at layers 12--15.
By layer 32, L2 divergence reaches: night (0.623) $>$ blur (0.544) $>$ fog (0.256) $>$ noise (0.147).
The distinction explains why early-layer cosine distance detection succeeds despite small absolute divergence: cosine distance is normalized, and the signal-to-noise ratio peaks at early layers where within-class variance is lowest.

\textbf{Peak amplification at layer 1.}
The amplification factor (corruption divergence / clean variance) peaks sharply at layer 1: night (212$\times$), blur (193$\times$), fog (41$\times$), noise (2.5$\times$).
This explains why layer 1 has the best separation ratio for detection.

\textbf{Cluster separability.}
Silhouette scores peak at layer 2 (0.833) and decrease monotonically deeper.
Nearest-neighbor classification accuracy is perfect (1.0) through layers 1--8, drops to 0.75 in middle layers, and partially recovers in late layers.

%==============================================================================
\section{Dual Failure Modes in VLA Actions}
\label{sec:failure_modes}
%==============================================================================

Detection alone does not characterize the downstream behavioral risk.
We identify two qualitatively distinct failure modes that different corruptions induce:

\textbf{Fog: Overconfident action collapse.}
Under fog, the model produces only 1.4 unique action tokens per dimension (vs.\ 6.7 clean), converging to nearly identical \emph{wrong} actions regardless of scene content.
Model confidence \emph{increases}---the model commits to the wrong action with high certainty.
This is the most dangerous failure mode: a confident wrong action that a robot would execute without hesitation.

\textbf{Night: Uncertainty explosion.}
Night corruption more than doubles action entropy from 1.66 nats/token (clean) to 3.42 nats (night).
The model becomes deeply uncertain, spreading probability across many tokens.
Unlike fog, night \emph{decreases} confidence, making this failure partially self-diagnosing.

These dual modes have fundamentally different safety profiles.
The hidden-state detector flags both (AUROC = 1.0 for both), but a complete safety system should distinguish between them---the overconfident collapse (fog) requires immediate intervention, while the uncertainty explosion (night) may allow for graceful degradation.

%==============================================================================
\section{Limitations and Failure Modes}
\label{sec:limitations}
%==============================================================================

We systematically characterize what the method \emph{cannot} detect, which is as important as what it can.

\textbf{Geometric and localized perturbations are invisible.}
None of the following perturbations are flagged as OOD: 1--1000 random pixel changes, channel swaps (GBR, BRG), horizontal flips, 90-degree rotations.
The cosine distances remain below the $3\sigma$ threshold in all cases.
This is a fundamental limitation: the detector captures \emph{distributional} corruption (global statistical changes to the image), not geometric transformations or localized edits.
We note that gradient-based adversarial attacks (e.g., PGD, FGSM) are not evaluated here and represent an important direction for future work.

\textbf{High-frequency noise evades detection.}
A high-frequency noise pattern (cosine distance $2.98 \times 10^{-5}$) falls below the threshold despite being visually dissimilar to clean images.
This mirrors the finding that Gaussian noise requires high severity for reliable detection.

\textbf{Corruption order matters.}
When two corruptions are applied sequentially, order can affect detectability.
In one case (blur followed by noise), the combined cosine distance ($4.45 \times 10^{-5}$) falls below threshold---a false negative.
The reverse order (noise followed by blur) is detected normally ($6.71 \times 10^{-3}$).

\textbf{Low-severity noise is undetectable.}
Gaussian noise below severity 0.75 has SNR $< 3$, meaning it is indistinguishable from the noise floor.
This is the hardest corruption for the detector across all experiments.

\textbf{Scope of evaluation.}
All results are from a single model (OpenVLA-7B) with synthetic corruptions applied to driving images using a fixed prompt.
While the mechanistic arguments (determinism, stable attention, distributed signal) should generalize, empirical validation on other VLA architectures and real-world corruption is needed.

\textbf{Partial corruption.}
On the positive side, even a 50$\times$50 pixel patch of corruption ($\sim$5\% of a 224$\times$224 image) produces cosine distance $6.1 \times 10^{-4}$---7.6$\times$ above threshold, reliably detected.

%==============================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%==============================================================================

\textbf{Why are the results so clean?}
The AUROC = 1.0 results may appear suspicious, but they follow necessarily from two properties: (1) model determinism in eval mode, which sets the in-distribution variance to essentially zero, and (2) the non-trivial embedding shift induced by visual corruptions.
With a noise floor of $\sim$$10^{-4}$ and corruption signals of $10^{-3}$--$10^{-2}$, perfect separation is the \emph{expected} outcome, not an anomalous one.
The more informative results are the \emph{severity gradients} (when does detection degrade?) and the \emph{failure modes} (what cannot be detected?).

\textbf{Compound corruptions.}
Real-world degradation involves simultaneous corruptions.
All tested compound scenarios (pairwise, triple, all-four) achieve AUROC = 1.0.
Compound distances are sub-additive: the dominant corruption determines the embedding direction while the weaker corruption modulates magnitude.
Fog + noise produces the lowest compound distance ($1.56 \times 10^{-3}$), as fog and noise have anti-correlated embedding shifts (cosine similarity $-0.53$).

\textbf{Corruption classification.}
The same embedding supports 4-way corruption type classification (centroid or direction-based) with 100\% accuracy at full severity and 96.9\% at severity 0.25 using direction projection.
Combined with severity estimation ($R^2 = 0.83$--$0.99$), this enables a complete monitoring pipeline: detect $\to$ classify type $\to$ estimate severity $\to$ trigger type-specific response (e.g., fog lights for fog, infrared switch for night).

\textbf{Temporal deployment.}
In frame-by-frame simulation (50 trials $\times$ 100 frames), the detector achieves zero false alarms on clean streams and detects corruption on the first corrupted frame with zero latency.
No temporal smoothing or aggregation is needed.

\textbf{Online adaptation.}
The centroid can be updated in a streaming fashion using an exponential moving average.
With $\alpha = 0.01$--$0.1$, the EMA centroid converges within $\sim$10 frames and maintains detection quality under gradual distribution shift.

%==============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%==============================================================================

We have shown that a simple, near-zero-cost monitoring signal---cosine distance of hidden-state embeddings from a clean centroid---provides perfect OOD detection for the OpenVLA-7B VLA model across four visual corruption types.
The method requires as few as 2 calibration images, adds 14.6\,$\mu$s overhead, and transfers across scenes without recalibration.
We provide a mechanistic account rooted in model determinism and stable attention patterns, and comprehensively characterize failure modes (geometric transformations, low-severity noise, specific corruption orderings).

Our results suggest that VLA models' internal representations are far more informative about input quality than their output distributions.
This principle---that \emph{hidden states know what confidence doesn't}---may extend to other safety-monitoring tasks in deployed AI systems.
Future work should validate across VLA architectures, evaluate on real-world corruption, and explore whether the method can detect semantic OOD shifts (novel objects, unusual scenes) beyond the distributional corruptions studied here.

\begin{itemize}

\subsection{Compound Corruption Results}
\label{app:compound}

All pairwise, triple, and all-four compound corruptions achieve AUROC = 1.0.
Compound distances are sub-additive: fog + noise = $1.56 \times 10^{-3}$ ($< $ fog alone at $3.06 \times 10^{-3}$).

\subsection{Online Calibration Adaptation}
\label{app:online}

The centroid can be updated online using exponential moving average (EMA):
$\boldsymbol{\mu}_{t+1} = (1 - \alpha) \boldsymbol{\mu}_t + \alpha \, \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}_{t+1})$.
With $\alpha = 0.01$--$0.1$, EMA centroids converge within $\sim$10 clean frames and maintain AUROC = 1.0 throughout deployment, including under gradual distribution shift scenarios.
Cold-start performance is robust: even with a single seed for initial centroid estimation, detection degrades gracefully before convergence.
