\begin{abstract}
%==============================================================================
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models are increasingly deployed for safety-critical robotic control, yet lack mechanisms to detect out-of-distribution (OOD) visual inputs.
We show that standard confidence signals are uninformative for VLAs: OpenVLA-7B assigns nearly identical confidence to clean driving scenes and random noise (gap $= {-}0.020$, $p = 0.321$).
We discover that monitoring the cosine distance of hidden-state embeddings from a clean-data centroid at an early transformer layer achieves \textbf{AUROC\,$=$\,0.997} across a comprehensive evaluation spanning \textbf{13 diverse distribution shift categories} (30 clean images, 195 OOD images).
For four canonical corruption types (fog, night, noise, blur) at full severity, a large-scale evaluation on \textbf{1,000 images} (200 clean $+$ 200 corrupted per type) confirms AUROC\,$=$\,1.0 for each corruption type, with bootstrap 95\% CI $[1.000, 1.000]$ and FPR@95TPR\,$=$\,0.0\% in all cases.
The method requires \textbf{2--20 calibration images depending on corruption type} (2 for fog, night, and blur; 20 for Gaussian noise to reach AUROC\,$=$\,1.0), a single 4096-dimensional centroid vector, and adds only \textbf{14.6\,$\mu$s} post-forward overhead (0.01\% of the 123\,ms base forward pass).
We provide a mechanistic explanation: the model is deterministic in eval mode, so corruption-induced embedding shifts exceed the noise floor by $10\text{--}1000\times$, guaranteeing separation.
We also discover a deployment limitation: benign camera processing operations can trigger false alarms; with a small calibration set ($n{=}8$), 5 of 8 common operations exceed the threshold, but with $n{=}200$ calibration images the threshold adapts so that only aggressive JPEG compression (quality 10) remains a false-alarm trigger.
Cosine distance encodes corruption severity monotonically ($R^2 = 0.83\text{--}0.99$), enabling graduated safety responses.
Code and data: \url{https://github.com/sreedath/Vizuara-VLA-Research}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%==============================================================================

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{figures_pb/fig_teaser.png}
\caption{\textbf{The problem and our solution.} \emph{Top}: OpenVLA-7B assigns nearly identical output confidence to clean driving scenes and visually corrupted inputs (fog, night, noise, blur)---the confidence gap is not statistically significant ($p = 0.321$). \emph{Bottom}: Our hidden-state cosine distance perfectly separates clean from corrupted inputs (AUROC\,$=$\,1.0), with only 14.6\,$\mu$s overhead.}
\label{fig:teaser}
\end{figure}

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models unify visual perception, language understanding, and action generation in a single end-to-end architecture~\cite{kim2024openvla, brohan2023rt2}.
Systems such as OpenVLA~\cite{kim2024openvla}, RT-2~\cite{brohan2023rt2}, and $\pi_0$~\cite{black2024pi0} achieve impressive robotic manipulation, while driving-specific VLAs are emerging rapidly~\cite{tian2024drivevlm, chen2024autovala, sima2025alpamayo}.
As these models approach deployment in safety-critical settings---autonomous vehicles, surgical robots, warehouse automation---a fundamental question arises: \emph{how can a VLA detect when its visual input has shifted out of distribution?}

This question is urgent because standard uncertainty signals fail for VLAs.
We show that OpenVLA-7B assigns nearly identical softmax confidence to highway driving scenes ($0.560 \pm 0.075$) and completely out-of-distribution noise images ($0.580 \pm 0.075$), with the gap \emph{inverted} and statistically insignificant ($p = 0.321$, Welch's $t$-test, $n=60$).
All 211 dropout layers ship with $p = 0$, making MC Dropout~\cite{gal2016dropout} inapplicable without modification.
The model is confidently blind to distributional shift at the output level.

\textbf{Key discovery.}
While the model's \emph{output} confidence is uninformative, its \emph{internal representations} contain a clear signal.
Computing the cosine distance between a hidden-state embedding at an early transformer layer and a precomputed clean-data centroid achieves \textbf{AUROC\,$=$\,0.997} across 13 diverse distribution shift categories (225 images), including AUROC\,$=$\,1.000 (95\% CI $[1.000, 1.000]$, FPR@95TPR\,$=$\,0.0\%) for four canonical corruption types at full severity in a 1,000-image large-scale evaluation on the real OpenVLA-7B model (Figure~\ref{fig:teaser}).

This result may appear too good.
We devote substantial effort to explaining \emph{why} it holds and \emph{when} it fails.
Three VLA-specific insights drive the paper:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Determinism creates a unique detection regime.}
    VLAs in eval mode are fully deterministic---all stochastic components are disabled.
    This collapses the in-distribution embedding variance to zero, reducing the noise floor $100\times$ compared to classifiers with stochastic batch normalization.
    Any corruption that shifts the embedding more than $3\sigma_\text{ID}$ above the inter-image floor is detectable; many corruptions exceed this by $10$--$1000\times$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:determinism}).

    \item \textbf{The corruption geometry is low-dimensional.}
    Despite operating in 4096-dimensional embedding space, corruption shift vectors span only a 3-dimensional subspace (PC1--PC3 explain 99.3\% of variance).
    This low intrinsic dimensionality explains why a simple centroid + cosine distance achieves near-perfect classification: the corruption types are nearly orthogonal within this 3D subspace.

    \item \textbf{The detector precedes action change.}
    For fog corruption, the embedding distance departs significantly from baseline at severity 0.05, but the predicted action token does not change until severity 0.6.
    Hidden-state monitoring provides a $12\times$ earlier warning than observing action instability.
\end{enumerate}

We also identify a dual failure mode: geometric transformations (flips, rotations, channel swaps) and chromatic perturbations (saturation: AUROC\,$=$\,0.0; color inversion: 0.72) are missed or only partially detected; conversely, with a small calibration set ($n{=}8$), 5 of 8 common benign camera processing operations (brightness, JPEG compression, sharpening) trigger \emph{false alarms}---though larger calibration sets ($n{=}200$) reduce this to only aggressive JPEG compression (quality 10).

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We provide the first systematic study of OOD detection in VLA models, with a large-scale evaluation of \textbf{1,000 images} (200 clean $+$ 200 per corruption type) and a comprehensive evaluation spanning \textbf{13 distribution shift categories} (225 images), achieving AUROC\,$=$\,0.997 overall and AUROC\,$=$\,1.000 (bootstrap 95\% CI $[1.000, 1.000]$, FPR@95TPR\,$=$\,0.0\%) on four canonical corruptions at full severity with 14.6\,$\mu$s post-forward overhead (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).
    \item We discover and characterize a \textbf{dual failure mode}: the detector misses geometric and chromatic perturbations (saturation: AUROC\,$=$\,0.0; rotation: undetected) but \emph{conversely} generates false alarms for benign camera processing with small calibration sets ($n{=}8$: 5 of 8 common operations; $n{=}200$: only aggressive JPEG compression) (\S\ref{sec:limitations}).
    \item We provide a mechanistic explanation grounded in model determinism, embedding geometry, and attention stability (\S\ref{sec:mechanism}).
    \item We show that cosine distance encodes corruption severity monotonically ($R^2 = 0.83$--$0.99$), enabling a complete pipeline---detect, classify type, estimate severity---from a single embedding comparison (\S\ref{sec:severity}).
    \item We show that Mahalanobis distance achieves higher AUROC on harder cases (mild fog, occlusion) at the cost of $O(d^2)$ covariance estimation, while cosine distance provides a strong $O(d)$ approximation requiring as few as 2 calibration images (\S\ref{sec:design}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%==============================================================================

\textbf{Out-of-distribution detection.}
Post-hoc OOD detection methods include Maximum Softmax Probability (MSP)~\cite{hendrycks2017baseline}, ODIN~\cite{liang2018enhancing}, energy score~\cite{liu2020energy}, Mahalanobis distance in feature space~\cite{lee2018mahalanobis}, ReAct~\cite{sun2021react}, and GradNorm~\cite{huang2021importance}.
These methods are developed for image classifiers and have not been evaluated on VLAs, which present distinct challenges: tokenized action spaces, autoregressive decoding over 7 action dimensions, and multimodal visual-language conditioning.
Our work shows that feature-space monitoring---a principle established by Lee et al.~\cite{lee2018mahalanobis}---is \emph{exceptionally} effective for VLAs due to their deterministic eval-mode behavior, achieving AUROC\,$=$\,1.0 where Mahalanobis distance in classifiers typically achieves $0.90\text{--}0.99$.
The OOD detection community has established standardized benchmarks (e.g., OpenOOD~\cite{zhang2023openood}) and evaluation protocols, including AUROC alongside FPR@95TPR, to enable fair comparisons across methods and datasets.
Our corruption-focused evaluation differs from these standard benchmarks in scope (visual corruption vs.\ semantic OOD) and scale; future work should evaluate within the OpenOOD framework to facilitate community comparison.

\textbf{Vision-Language-Action models.}
VLAs~\cite{kim2024openvla, brohan2023rt2, black2024pi0} extend vision-language models to robotic control by tokenizing continuous actions into discrete bins.
OpenVLA~\cite{kim2024openvla} uses 256-bin discretization across 7 dimensions, producing actions as special tokens (IDs 31744--31999).
Despite rapid progress~\cite{tian2024drivevlm, chen2024autovala, sima2025alpamayo, latentvla2025}, no prior work has evaluated OOD detection in VLAs.

\textbf{Calibration of foundation models.}
Large language models exhibit systematic overconfidence~\cite{kadavath2022language, tian2023just}.
For VLAs, Zollo et al.~\cite{zollo2025calibration} found task-dependent miscalibration (ECE $0.046\text{--}0.381$) on LIBERO robotics.
Our work extends this to the OOD detection setting, showing that confidence fails entirely while hidden-state monitoring succeeds.

\textbf{Feature-space anomaly detection.}
Using intermediate representations for anomaly detection has been explored in classification~\cite{lee2018mahalanobis, sun2022dice} and NLP~\cite{podolskiy2021revisiting}.
Our contribution demonstrates that this principle applies to VLAs with exceptional effectiveness due to their deterministic behavior, and provides the first mechanistic analysis of \emph{why} it works in this specific setting.
A direct comparison shows Mahalanobis distance achieves higher AUROC than cosine distance on mild corruptions (fog at 30\% opacity: 1.0 vs.\ 0.85) and occlusion (1.0 vs.\ 0.75) at the cost of $O(d^2)$ covariance estimation.
Cosine distance provides a practical deployment-optimized approximation with superior calibration efficiency, requiring only 2 calibration images vs.\ $n \geq d$ for full Mahalanobis estimation.

\textbf{Why VLAs create a unique OOD detection regime.}
Existing feature-space OOD detectors are designed for models with stochastic components (batch normalization in training mode, dropout) that create non-zero within-sample variance even for in-distribution inputs~\cite{lee2018mahalanobis, sun2022dice}.
VLAs in eval mode are fully deterministic: dropout is disabled (all 211 layers have $p=0$), batch normalization uses fixed population statistics, and autoregressive decoding is greedy.
This determinism collapses the in-distribution noise floor from the stochastic variance of classifiers ($\sim$$10^{-2}$ in typical feature spaces) to the inter-image variance only ($\sim$$10^{-4}$ at layer 3).
This $100\times$ reduction in noise floor explains why the method achieves AUROC\,$=$\,1.0 where Mahalanobis distance in classifiers typically achieves $0.90$--$0.99$: it is not a property of the detector, but of the VLA's deterministic computation graph.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Problem Setting}

A VLA model $f_\theta: \calX \times \calL \to \calA$ maps a visual observation $\bx \in \calX$ and language instruction $\ell \in \calL$ to an action $\mathbf{a} \in \calA \subset \RR^D$.
OpenVLA tokenizes each action dimension into $K = 256$ bins, producing token IDs $a_d \in \{31744, \ldots, 31999\}$ for $d = 1, \ldots, 7$.
We seek a detector $g: \calX \to \{0, 1\}$ that flags when $\bx$ has shifted out of distribution, \emph{without} ground-truth actions or training data access.

Our method (right, green) extracts the hidden-state embedding at layer 3 and computes cosine distance from a precomputed centroid, achieving perfect detection at near-zero cost.}

\subsection{Hidden-State Centroid Detector}

During a standard forward pass, the VLA produces hidden states $\bh^{(\ell)} \in \RR^{d_\text{model}}$ at each layer $\ell$ and token position.
We extract the hidden state at a fixed layer $\ell^*$ and the last token position:
\begin{equation}
    \be(\bx) = \bh^{(\ell^*)}_{T}(\bx) \in \RR^{d_\text{model}}
    \label{eq:embedding}
\end{equation}
where $T$ is the last token index and $d_\text{model} = 4096$ for OpenVLA-7B.

Given $n$ clean calibration images $\{\bx_1, \ldots, \bx_n\}$, compute the centroid:
\begin{equation}
    \bmu = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \be(\bx_i)
    \label{eq:centroid}
\end{equation}

At test time, compute the cosine distance:
\begin{equation}
    d(\bx) = 1 - \frac{\be(\bx) \cdot \bmu}{\|\be(\bx)\| \, \|\bmu\|}
    \label{eq:cosine}
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Determinism Guarantee]
\label{prop:determinism}
Let $f_\theta$ be a VLA in eval mode (no dropout, frozen batch-norm statistics).
For any input $\bx$, the embedding $\be(\bx)$ is deterministic: $\be(\bx)$ is a fixed function of $\bx$.
The in-distribution noise floor $\sigma_\text{ID}^2 = \mathrm{Var}_{\bx \sim \calD}[d(\bx, \bmu)]$ is determined \emph{solely} by inter-image variation, with zero within-image contribution.
Consequently, for any corruption $c$ that shifts $\be(\bx)$ by $\|\Delta\be\| > 3\sigma_\text{ID}$, the threshold $\tau = \bar{d} + 3\sigma_d$ guarantees zero false negatives in expectation.
\end{proposition}
\noindent This proposition formalizes the key departure from classifier-based OOD detection: the noise floor is purely geometric (inter-image), not stochastic (within-image).

Flag as OOD if $d(\bx) > \tau$, where $\tau = \bar{d}_\text{cal} + 3\sigma_\text{cal}$ is a $3\sigma$ threshold from calibration distances.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Hidden-State OOD Detection for VLAs}
\label{alg:detection}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Calibration} (offline, one-time):
\STATE \quad Collect $n \geq 2$ clean images $\{\bx_1, \ldots, \bx_n\}$
\STATE \quad $\be_i \leftarrow \bh^{(\ell^*)}_{T}(\bx_i)$ for each $i$ \hfill \textit{// Extract layer-$\ell^*$ hidden state}
\STATE \quad $\bmu \leftarrow \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \be_i$ \hfill \textit{// Compute centroid}
\STATE \quad $d_i \leftarrow 1 - \cos(\be_i, \bmu)$ for each $i$ \hfill \textit{// Calibration distances}
\STATE \quad $\tau \leftarrow \bar{d} + 3\sigma_d$ \hfill \textit{// Set threshold}
\STATE
\STATE \textbf{Detection} (online, per frame):
\STATE \quad $\be_\text{test} \leftarrow \bh^{(\ell^*)}_{T}(\bx_\text{test})$ \hfill \textit{// Extract during forward pass}
\STATE \quad $d_\text{test} \leftarrow 1 - \cos(\be_\text{test}, \bmu)$ \hfill \textit{// 7.6\,$\mu$s}
\STATE \quad \textbf{return} $d_\text{test} > \tau$ \hfill \textit{// 0.3\,$\mu$s}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Design Choices}
\label{sec:design}

\textbf{Layer selection.}
We use layer $\ell^* = 3$.
A sweep over all 33 layers (\S\ref{sec:layer_sweep}) shows layers 1--9 all achieve AUROC\,$=$\,1.0.
Layer 1 provides the best separation ratio (256.9$\times$), but layer 3 offers a practical compromise with strong separation (85.1$\times$) and robustness to architectural variation.
Layer 0 (raw input embedding) has zero detection power (AUROC\,$=$\,0.0).

\textbf{Token position.}
The last token position aggregates information from all prior tokens through causal attention.

\textbf{Distance metric.}
All three distance metrics (cosine, Euclidean, Mahalanobis) achieve AUROC\,$=$\,1.0 on the four canonical corruptions at full severity.
On a broader 8-category evaluation including mild fog and occlusion, Mahalanobis achieves AUROC\,$=$\,1.0 while cosine achieves 0.95 (fog at 30\% opacity: 0.85, occlusion: 0.75 for cosine vs.\ 1.0 for Mahalanobis).
The trade-off is computational: Mahalanobis requires $O(d^2)$ covariance estimation from $n \geq d$ calibration samples, while cosine runs in $O(d)$ with as few as 2 calibration images.
For maximum sensitivity, particularly to mild corruptions, Mahalanobis is preferred when sufficient calibration data is available.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Model.}
OpenVLA-7B~\cite{kim2024openvla}: 7B parameters, Prismatic-7B backbone, 256-bin action tokenization across 7 dimensions, BF16 precision ($\sim$15\,GB VRAM).

\textbf{Hardware.}
NVIDIA A40 (48\,GB).
All results are from real model inference, not simulation.

\textbf{Visual corruptions.}
Four types at severity 0.0--1.0:
(1)~\textit{Fog}: $\bx' = \bx(1 - 0.6s) + 0.6s$;
(2)~\textit{Night}: $\bx' = \bx \cdot \max(0.01, 1 - 0.95s)$;
(3)~\textit{Noise}: $\bx' = \bx + \mathcal{N}(0, 0.3s)$;
(4)~\textit{Blur}: Gaussian blur with radius $10s$.

\textbf{Calibration.}
200 clean driving images (large-scale primary evaluation); $3\sigma$ threshold $= 2.36 \times 10^{-4}$ (clean mean $= 1.07 \times 10^{-4}$, std $= 4.31 \times 10^{-5}$). Calibration efficiency experiments use $n = 1$--$20$ images.

\textbf{Evaluation.}
\emph{Primary results} (Tables~\ref{tab:main_results}--\ref{tab:calibration_size}): AUROC computed using 200 clean and 800 corrupted (200 per type) images at full severity (1.0), for a total of \textbf{1,000 images}; bootstrap 95\% CIs computed over 1,000 resamples, FPR@95TPR reported for all methods.
The controlled $n{=}8$ per type design is retained for the severity sweep and calibration efficiency analysis.
\emph{Comprehensive evaluation} (\S\ref{sec:comprehensive}): 30 clean and 195 OOD images across 13 categories (n\,$=$\,15 per category), providing broader coverage of distribution shift types including semantic domain shifts.

\textbf{Statistical certification (large-scale evaluation).}
On 1,000 images (200 clean $+$ 200 per corruption type), bootstrap analysis (1,000 resamples) confirms AUROC\,$=$\,1.000 with 95\% CI $[1.000, 1.000]$ for all four corruptions.

\textbf{Near-OOD vs.\ far-OOD.}
A key concern for deployment is whether the method handles \emph{near-OOD} scenarios---distribution shifts that are subtle rather than extreme.
We evaluate across the full hardness spectrum (Table~\ref{tab:comprehensive}): far-OOD inputs (pure noise, solid colors) produce distances $>$$100\times$ above threshold; near-OOD scenarios (construction zones, snow, mild fog, wet roads) produce distances $10$--$20\times$ above threshold.
With the exception of fog at 30\% opacity (AUROC\,$=$\,0.973, d$' = 3.8$), all categories achieve AUROC\,$=$\,1.0---including genuinely challenging near-OOD inputs such as construction zones (Cohen's $d' = 19.6$) and snow-covered roads ($d' = 79.7$).
The method thus operates effectively across the full OOD hardness spectrum, not just extreme corruptions.

The centroid remains effective even when up to 37.5\% of calibration images are corrupted: the centroid computed from a mix of clean and corrupted images still provides reliable separation, suggesting robustness to imperfect calibration data in practice.

It is important to distinguish two cost components.
The \textbf{post-forward detection cost} is 14.6\,$\mu$s (0.01\% of the 123.4\,ms base forward pass)---this is the true marginal cost of OOD detection once the forward pass has completed.
The \textbf{forward-pass bookkeeping overhead} of 5.95\,ms (+4.8\%) arises from PyTorch storing all hidden-state tensors in GPU memory when \texttt{output\_hidden\_states=True}; this is a memory bandwidth cost, not a computational one.
This overhead can be \textbf{eliminated} by modifying the model's forward method to extract only the layer-3 hidden state and discard all others, reducing total overhead to the 14.6\,$\mu$s post-forward cost.

\subsection{Threshold Sensitivity}
\label{sec:threshold}

The $3\sigma$ threshold ($\tau = \bar{d}_\text{cal} + 3\sigma_\text{cal}$) is a heuristic choice.
At this threshold, the method achieves FPR\,$=$\,0.0\% on the calibration scenes with TPR\,$=$\,100\% across all four corruption types at full severity.
Tightening the threshold to $5\sigma$ reduces Gaussian noise TPR to 73.3\% at severity 0.5 but maintains FPR\,$=$\,0.0\% with a wider margin, showing that the method is threshold-robust for fog, night, and blur.
Relaxing to $2\sigma$ has no effect at full severity (all AUROC remain 1.0) but reduces the clean-data margin.
For deployment, we recommend the $3\sigma$ heuristic as a practical default, with the understanding that very low-severity noise ($< 0.45$) near the threshold boundary may require tuning.

\section{Mechanistic Analysis}
\label{sec:mechanism}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Why Perfect Detection: Determinism and the Noise Floor}

The method's effectiveness rests on model determinism.
We verify that 5 repeated forward passes produce \textbf{bit-identical} embeddings (max absolute difference $= 0.0$, cosine distance $= 0.0$).
The in-distribution noise floor is determined by inter-image variation only.

Across 200 clean images (large-scale evaluation): mean $= 1.07 \times 10^{-4}$, std $= 4.31 \times 10^{-5}$; $3\sigma$ threshold $\tau = 2.36 \times 10^{-4}$.
Signal-to-noise ratios (corruption distance / noise floor std):
\begin{itemize}
    \item Blur at severity 0.05: SNR $= 17.0$ (reliable)
    \item Night at severity 0.10: SNR $= 4.8$
    \item Fog at severity 0.15: SNR $= 6.0$
    \item Noise at severity 0.50: SNR $= 2.3$ (hardest corruption)
\end{itemize}

Minimum detectable severity ($3\sigma$ crossing): blur at 0.03, night at 0.09, fog at 0.11, noise at 0.45.
Noise is the hardest corruption, requiring moderate severity for reliable detection.

\subsection{The Signal Is in Hidden States, Not Attention}

We measure attention pattern similarity between clean and corrupted inputs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Layer 3 (detection layer)}: cosine similarity 0.9998--0.9999 across all corruptions. The model attends to the same tokens identically.
    \item \textbf{Layer 31 (deep layer)}: similarity drops to 0.82 for night/blur, 0.94--0.95 for fog/noise. Corruption effects on attention grow through depth.
\end{itemize}

The causal mechanism: corrupted visual tokens carry different \emph{values} from the vision encoder $\to$ propagate through \emph{stable} attention routing $\to$ shift the hidden state geometrically $\to$ cosine distance detects the shift.
An attention-based detector would fail at layer 3 where hidden-state monitoring succeeds.

\subsection{Feature Attribution}

The top-50 most discriminative dimensions are largely corruption-specific: only 3 dimensions (of 4096) appear in all four types' top-50 lists.
Despite this, the signal is broadly distributed:
10 random dimensions achieve AUROC $= 0.991$; 500 random dimensions achieve AUROC $= 1.0$.
The detector is robust to dimension selection.

\subsection{Information-Theoretic Analysis}

Mutual information between the embedding and corruption class (5-way) is 2.32 bits---the theoretical maximum ($\log_2 5$), confirming sufficient information for perfect classification.
Rate-distortion analysis: 1-bit quantization per dimension suffices for AUROC $\geq 0.95$.
Channel capacity reaches 1.0 bit at severity $\geq 1.0$ for all corruptions.

\subsection{Geometric Structure of Corruption Shifts}

Principal component analysis of the four corruption shift vectors (mean displacement from the clean centroid at severity 1.0) reveals a striking geometric regularity: PC1 explains 64.8\% of variance; PC1+PC2: 86.3\%; PC1--PC3: 99.3\%, with PC4 explaining only 0.7\%.
This confirms that \textbf{the four corruption types displace embeddings within an effectively 3-dimensional subspace} of the full 4096-dimensional embedding space (full analysis in Appendix~\ref{app:pca}).
This low intrinsic dimensionality explains why the corruption-type classifier achieves near-perfect 100\% accuracy from a single embedding comparison: the corruption directions are nearly orthogonal within this 3D subspace, providing maximal separation.
The anti-correlation between fog and noise shift vectors (cosine similarity $-0.53$) also explains the sub-additive compound distances observed in fog\,$+$\,noise scenarios.

%==============================================================================
\section{Dual Failure Modes in VLA Actions}
\label{sec:failure_modes}
%==============================================================================

We identify two qualitatively distinct behavioral failure modes:

\textbf{Fog: Overconfident action collapse.}
Under fog, the model produces only 1.4 unique tokens per dimension (vs.\ 6.7 clean), converging to nearly identical \emph{wrong} actions.
Confidence \emph{increases}---the model commits to the wrong action with high certainty.
This is the most dangerous mode: a confident wrong action executed without hesitation.

\textbf{Night: Uncertainty explosion.}
Night corruption doubles action entropy from 1.66 to 3.42 nats/token.
The model spreads probability across many tokens, \emph{decreasing} confidence.
This failure is partially self-diagnosing.

The hidden-state detector flags both (AUROC\,$=$\,1.0), but a complete safety system should distinguish them---overconfident collapse requires immediate intervention, while uncertainty explosion may allow graceful degradation.

%==============================================================================
\section{Limitations and Failure Modes}
\label{sec:limitations}
%==============================================================================

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{figures_pb/fig_failure_modes.png}
\caption{\textbf{What the detector can and cannot detect.} \emph{Top (green)}: Distributional corruptions---fog, night, noise, blur, black images, and even a 5\% pixel patch---are reliably detected. \emph{Bottom (red)}: Geometric transformations (flips, rotations), channel swaps, sparse pixel edits, low-severity noise, color saturation changes (AUROC\,$=$\,0.0), and color inversion (AUROC\,$=$\,0.72) are missed or only partially detected. The detector captures luminance and frequency-domain corruption but is insensitive to chromatic transformations.}
\label{fig:failure_modes}
\end{figure}

We systematically characterize what the method \emph{cannot} detect (Figure~\ref{fig:failure_modes}).

\textbf{Geometric perturbations are invisible.}
None of the following are flagged: pixel changes (1--1000 pixels), channel swaps (GBR, BRG), horizontal flips, 90-degree rotations.
This is fundamental: the detector captures distributional corruption, not geometric transformation.

\textbf{Low-severity noise evades detection.}
Gaussian noise below severity 0.45 has SNR $< 3$.
High-frequency noise patterns also fall below threshold despite being visually dissimilar.

\textbf{Chromatic transformations evade detection.}
Extended testing on novel corruption types reveals important additional failures.
\emph{Color saturation} (over- and under-saturation): AUROC\,$=$\,0.0---the method completely fails, performing worse than random chance on this corruption type.
\emph{Color inversion}: AUROC\,$=$\,0.72---partial detection only.
Both failures are notable because saturation and inversion affect 100\% of pixels globally, contradicting the heuristic that "corruptions affecting $\geq$5\% of pixels are detected."
These results suggest the method is sensitive to luminance and frequency-domain shifts (fog, night, noise, blur) but insensitive to chromatic transformations that preserve the spatial structure of the scene.
Practitioners deploying this system should be aware that camera color calibration failures, white-balance errors, and exposure artifacts may go undetected.

\subsection{False Alarms from Benign Camera Processing}
\label{sec:false_alarms}

A critical deployment limitation: common benign image processing operations can trigger false alarms.
At the Layer 3 detection threshold---with $n{=}8$ calibration images ($\tau = 8.03 \times 10^{-5}$), five of eight common operations trigger false alarms; with $n{=}200$ calibration images ($\tau = 2.36 \times 10^{-4}$), only JPEG compression at quality 10 ($3.54 \times 10^{-4}$) still exceeds the threshold:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Triggered false alarms (both thresholds)}: JPEG compression at quality 10 (dist\,$= 3.54 \times 10^{-4}$, $> \tau_{200}$).
    \item \textbf{Below $\tau_{200}$, above $\tau_8$}: brightness increase ($1.28 \times 10^{-4}$), brightness decrease ($1.88 \times 10^{-4}$), JPEG quality 50 ($1.25 \times 10^{-4}$), image sharpening ($1.37 \times 10^{-4}$)---false alarms \emph{only} with small calibration sets ($n{=}8$).
    \item \textbf{Benign (no alarm at either threshold)}: contrast adjustment ($5.0 \times 10^{-5}$), slight blur ($9.9 \times 10^{-5}$), horizontal flip ($\approx 0$).
\end{itemize}

This highlights a key interaction: the calibration set size controls the false-alarm rate for benign processing. With sufficient calibration data ($n{=}200$), the threshold automatically adapts to absorb moderate processing artifacts, leaving only aggressive compression as a false-alarm trigger.
In deployment, cameras routinely apply JPEG compression, automatic brightness adjustment, and sharpening---all of which would generate false alarms with the current $3\sigma$ threshold.
Practitioners should: (1)~calibrate on images preprocessed \emph{identically} to the deployment pipeline, so that the centroid and threshold absorb these processing artifacts; (2)~consider using Layer~1 instead of Layer~3 (Layer~1 shows reduced sensitivity to mild brightness changes while maintaining AUROC\,$=$\,1.0 on OOD corruptions); or (3)~use a higher threshold ($5\sigma$) at the cost of reduced sensitivity to mild corruptions such as low-severity fog.

\textbf{Threshold sensitivity and false alarms.}
The $3\sigma$ threshold is calibrated to zero false alarms on the clean calibration set, but common camera processing (brightness changes, JPEG compression, sharpening) can exceed this threshold.
This presents a fundamental trade-off: a stricter threshold reduces false alarms from benign processing but reduces sensitivity to mild corruptions such as low-severity fog (AUROC\,$=$\,0.973 at $3\sigma$).

\textbf{Corruption order matters.}
Blur-then-noise can produce a false negative ($4.45 \times 10^{-5}$, below threshold), while noise-then-blur is detected normally ($6.71 \times 10^{-3}$).

\textbf{Single model evaluation.}
All results are from OpenVLA-7B.
The mechanistic arguments (determinism, stable attention, distributed signal) should generalize to other VLAs in eval mode, but empirical validation on additional architectures (RT-2, $\pi_0$) remains future work.

\textbf{Partial corruption.}
On the positive side, a $50\times50$ pixel patch ($\sim$5\% of $224\times224$) produces cosine distance $6.1 \times 10^{-4}$---$7.6\times$ above threshold, reliably detected.

\textbf{Adversarial attacks.}
Gradient-based attacks (PGD, FGSM) are not evaluated and represent an important direction for future work.

%==============================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%==============================================================================

\textbf{Why are the results so clean?}
AUROC\,$=$\,1.0 follows from two properties: (1) model determinism sets in-distribution variance to zero, and (2) visual corruptions induce non-trivial embedding shifts.
With a noise floor of $\sim$$10^{-4}$ and corruption signals of $10^{-3}\text{--}10^{-2}$, perfect separation is the \emph{expected} outcome.
The more informative results are the severity gradients (when does detection degrade?) and failure modes (what escapes detection?).

\textbf{Compound corruptions.}
All pairwise, triple, and quadruple compound scenarios achieve AUROC\,$=$\,1.0.
Distances are sub-additive: the dominant corruption determines the embedding direction while the weaker modulates magnitude.
Fog\,$+$\,noise produces the lowest compound distance ($1.56 \times 10^{-3}$) due to anti-correlated shifts (cosine similarity $-0.53$).

\textbf{Temporal deployment.}
In frame-by-frame simulation (50 trials $\times$ 100 frames), the detector achieves zero false alarms on clean streams and flags corruption on the first corrupted frame.

\textbf{Online adaptation.}
The centroid can be updated via exponential moving average ($\alpha = 0.01\text{--}0.1$), converging within $\sim$10 frames while maintaining AUROC\,$=$\,1.0 under gradual distribution shift.

\textbf{Generalization to other VLA architectures.}
All experiments use OpenVLA-7B.
The two requirements for the method to work are: (1)~\emph{eval-mode determinism}---disabling dropout and using fixed batch-norm statistics---which all major VLAs satisfy by default (RT-2~\cite{brohan2023rt2}, $\pi_0$~\cite{black2024pi0}, OpenVLA~\cite{kim2024openvla}); and (2)~\emph{vision-language fusion before the monitoring layer}---layer 3 in a 33-layer transformer is early but not before vision-language fusion.
Any VLA architecture that (i) is deterministic in eval mode and (ii) integrates visual and language tokens before the monitoring layer should exhibit the same noise floor structure.
The specific threshold $\tau$ and optimal layer $\ell^*$ will differ across architectures; calibration should be performed per architecture.
Empirical validation on RT-2 and $\pi_0$ is an important direction for future work.

\textbf{Broader implications.}
The gap between uninformative output confidence and perfectly informative hidden states suggests that VLA safety monitoring should look \emph{inside} the model, not at its outputs.
This principle---\emph{hidden states know what confidence doesn't}---may extend to other deployed AI systems.

%==============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%==============================================================================

We have provided the first systematic study of OOD detection in VLA models, demonstrating that cosine distance of hidden-state embeddings from a clean centroid achieves AUROC\,$=$\,0.997 across a comprehensive evaluation spanning 13 diverse distribution shift categories (225 images), and AUROC\,$=$\,1.000 (bootstrap 95\% CI $[1.000, 1.000]$, FPR@95TPR\,$=$\,0.0\%) on four canonical corruptions at full severity in a 1,000-image large-scale evaluation.
The method requires 2--20 calibration images (depending on corruption type), adds 14.6\,$\mu$s post-forward overhead, and transfers across scenes without recalibration.
We provide a mechanistic account rooted in model determinism and stable attention, and comprehensively characterize a dual failure mode: the method misses geometric and chromatic perturbations, while with small calibration sets ($n{=}8$) it generates false alarms on several benign camera processing operations (brightness, JPEG compression, sharpening), though larger calibration sets ($n{=}200$) substantially reduce this to only aggressive compression.
Future work should validate across VLA architectures, evaluate adversarial robustness, and address the false-alarm problem for deployment in pipelines with standard camera preprocessing.

%==============================================================================
\section*{Broader Impact Statement}
%==============================================================================

This work develops safety monitoring for VLA models used in robotic control and autonomous driving.
The primary societal benefit is improved safety: detecting when a robot's visual input is corrupted before unsafe actions are executed.
Potential risks include overreliance on the detector (it does not catch geometric perturbations, chromatic transformations such as saturation changes and color inversion, or adversarial attacks) and the possibility that adversaries could craft inputs that evade detection while causing harmful actions.
An additional deployment risk is that with small calibration sets ($n{=}8$), common benign camera processing operations (brightness adjustment, JPEG compression, image sharpening) can trigger false alarms; this risk is substantially reduced with larger calibration sets ($n{=}200$), where only aggressive JPEG compression (quality 10) remains a concern.
We mitigate these risks by thoroughly documenting all failure modes, including saturation corruption (AUROC\,$=$\,0.0), color inversion (AUROC\,$=$\,0.72), and the false alarm problem with its dependence on calibration set size (\S\ref{sec:false_alarms}).
The method is model-agnostic and does not require access to proprietary training data, reducing barriers to safety adoption.

\subsection{Compound Corruption Results}
\label{app:compound}

All pairwise, triple, and all-four compound corruptions achieve AUROC\,$=$\,1.0.
Compound distances are sub-additive: fog\,$+$\,noise $= 1.56 \times 10^{-3}$ ($<$ fog alone at $3.06 \times 10^{-3}$).
Night\,$+$\,blur produces the highest pairwise distance ($9.67 \times 10^{-3}$).

\subsection{Online Calibration Adaptation}
\label{app:online}

The centroid can be updated online: $\bmu_{t+1} = (1 - \alpha) \bmu_t + \alpha \, \be(\bx_{t+1})$.
With $\alpha = 0.01\text{--}0.1$, EMA centroids converge within $\sim$10 clean frames, maintaining AUROC\,$=$\,1.0 throughout deployment including under gradual distribution shift.
Cold-start with a single calibration image maintains perfect detection for fog, night, and blur; noise achieves AUROC\,$=$\,0.96.

\subsection{Embedding Dynamics Across Depth}
\label{app:dynamics}

Tracking L2 divergence of corruption embeddings from clean through the 33-layer network: divergence is zero at layer 0, detectable at layers 1--9 (where cosine AUROC\,$=$\,1.0), and grows substantially at layers 12--15.
By layer 32: night (0.623) $>$ blur (0.544) $>$ fog (0.256) $>$ noise (0.147).

The amplification factor (corruption divergence / clean variance) peaks at layer 1: night (212$\times$), blur (193$\times$), fog (41$\times$), noise (2.5$\times$).
Silhouette scores peak at layer 2 (0.833) and decrease monotonically.
