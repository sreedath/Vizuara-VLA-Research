\begin{abstract}
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models are rapidly emerging as unified architectures for autonomous driving, jointly processing visual observations, linguistic instructions, and action generation. However, a critical gap exists: \emph{these models lack reliable uncertainty quantification}, producing confident predictions even in novel or dangerous scenarios where they are likely to fail. We introduce \textbf{CalibDrive}, the first systematic benchmark for evaluating the calibration of driving VLAs across 8 scenario categories spanning routine highway driving to safety-critical long-tail events. Our analysis of OpenVLA-7B reveals severe miscalibration: the baseline model exhibits an Expected Calibration Error (ECE) of 0.399, with catastrophic miscalibration (ECE $>$ 0.70) in long-tail scenarios such as emergency vehicles and occluded agents, where the model is confidently wrong in 100\% of cases. We evaluate five uncertainty quantification methods---MC Dropout, Deep Ensembles, Temperature Scaling, Conformal Prediction, and a novel Prompt Ensemble approach---and propose \textbf{CalibDrive-Combined}, a hybrid method that combines ensemble disagreement with conformal coverage guarantees and Platt-scaled confidence mapping. MC Dropout achieves the largest calibration improvement (58\% ECE reduction), while Conformal Prediction achieves perfect failure detection (AUROC = 1.0). Through selective prediction, calibrated VLAs reduce failure rates by 43\% at 50\% coverage, demonstrating that uncertainty-aware VLAs can identify their own failure modes and abstain in dangerous situations. Critically, we discover that OpenVLA-7B assigns nearly identical confidence to highway driving and completely out-of-distribution noise images---large-scale validation ($N$=200) reveals the gap is \emph{inverted} ($\Delta = -0.020$) and not statistically significant ($p = 0.321$). The model ships with all 211 dropout layers set to $p = 0$, making standard MC Dropout inapplicable without modification. Most significantly, we discover that \emph{cosine distance} in the model's hidden state space provides near-universal OOD detection (AUROC = 0.979) across both pixel-level and semantic OOD, requiring only 25 calibration images and a single centroid vector. Code and benchmark are available at \url{https://github.com/sreedath/Vizuara-VLA-Research}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%==============================================================================

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models represent a paradigm shift in autonomous driving, unifying visual perception, natural language reasoning, and continuous action generation within a single end-to-end architecture \cite{kim2024openvla, tian2024drivevlm, chen2024autovala}. Recent systems such as DriveVLM \cite{tian2024drivevlm}, AutoVLA \cite{chen2024autovala}, Alpamayo-R1 \cite{sima2025alpamayo}, and ELF-VLA \cite{elf2025vla} demonstrate impressive performance across complex driving scenarios, often matching or exceeding modular pipeline approaches on standard benchmarks like NAVSIM \cite{dauner2024navsim} and nuScenes \cite{caesar2020nuscenes}.

However, a fundamental question remains unanswered: \emph{when these models predict a trajectory, how confident should we be in that prediction?} Current driving VLAs produce point predictions without any calibrated measure of uncertainty. This is particularly dangerous in safety-critical deployment, where a model that is confidently wrong in an unfamiliar scenario---such as encountering an emergency vehicle, an occluded pedestrian, or unusual road debris---can lead to catastrophic failures.

The need for calibrated uncertainty in driving models is well-established in the classical autonomous driving literature \cite{feng2021review, mcallister2017concrete}, yet the unique challenges of VLA architectures---which tokenize continuous actions into discrete bins and autoregressively decode multi-dimensional trajectories---create novel calibration challenges that have not been studied. Only one prior work \cite{zollo2025calibration} has investigated VLA calibration, focusing exclusively on tabletop robotics (LIBERO benchmark), finding ECE values ranging from 0.046 to 0.381 across tasks. No study has examined VLA calibration in the safety-critical driving domain.

\textbf{Contributions.} We make the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{CalibDrive Benchmark}: The first systematic benchmark for evaluating VLA calibration in autonomous driving, spanning 8 scenario categories across 3 difficulty levels with standardized metrics (Section~\ref{sec:benchmark}).

    \item \textbf{Miscalibration Analysis}: A comprehensive study revealing that driving VLAs are severely miscalibrated (ECE = 0.399 overall), with catastrophic miscalibration (ECE $>$ 0.70) in long-tail safety-critical scenarios (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).

    \item \textbf{UQ Method Evaluation}: Systematic comparison of five uncertainty quantification methods adapted for driving VLAs, including a novel Prompt Ensemble approach that exploits VLAs' language conditioning (Section~\ref{sec:methods}).

    \item \textbf{CalibDrive-Combined}: A hybrid method combining ensemble disagreement, MC Dropout variance, and conformal coverage guarantees with Platt-scaled confidence mapping (Section~\ref{sec:combined}).

    \item \textbf{Selective Prediction}: Demonstration that calibrated VLAs can reduce failure rates by 43\% through uncertainty-aware abstention, with implications for safe deployment (Section~\ref{sec:selective}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Vision-Language-Action Models for Driving}

VLA models for autonomous driving can be categorized into three generations. \textbf{Modular VLAs} like DriveVLM \cite{tian2024drivevlm} and InsightDrive \cite{insightdrive2025} use VLMs for scene understanding while maintaining separate planning modules. \textbf{End-to-end VLAs} such as OpenVLA \cite{kim2024openvla}, AutoVLA \cite{chen2024autovala}, and LatentVLA \cite{latentvla2025} directly output control actions from multimodal inputs. \textbf{Reasoning-augmented VLAs} like Alpamayo-R1 \cite{sima2025alpamayo} and CoC-VLA \cite{cocvla2025} incorporate chain-of-thought reasoning for complex scenarios. Despite rapid progress in prediction accuracy, none of these models provide calibrated uncertainty estimates.

\subsection{Uncertainty Quantification in Deep Learning}

Established UQ methods include MC Dropout \cite{gal2016dropout}, which approximates Bayesian inference through stochastic dropout at test time; Deep Ensembles \cite{lakshminarayanan2017simple}, which capture epistemic uncertainty through model diversity; Temperature Scaling \cite{guo2017calibration}, a post-hoc recalibration via a learned temperature parameter; and Conformal Prediction \cite{angelopoulos2023conformal}, which provides distribution-free coverage guarantees. While extensively studied for classification and regression, their adaptation to the autoregressive, tokenized action space of VLAs remains unexplored.

\subsection{Calibration of Foundation Models}

Recent work on LLM calibration \cite{kadavath2022language, tian2023just} reveals that larger language models tend to be better calibrated on factual questions but exhibit systematic overconfidence on adversarial or out-of-distribution inputs. For VLMs, calibration studies \cite{chen2024vlm_calib} show similar overconfidence patterns. For VLAs specifically, Zollo et al.~\cite{zollo2025calibration} provide the only calibration study to date, evaluating on the LIBERO robotics benchmark and finding task-dependent miscalibration. Our work extends this to the safety-critical driving domain with more diverse scenarios and a broader set of UQ methods.

\subsection{Safety in Autonomous Driving}

Selective prediction \cite{geifman2017selective, el2010foundations} enables models to abstain from making predictions when uncertain. In driving, this corresponds to requesting human intervention or executing a minimal risk condition (MRC). Prior work on safe driving under uncertainty \cite{feng2021review, mcallister2017concrete} has focused on classical perception pipelines; we extend selective prediction to VLA-based driving systems.

\section{CalibDrive Benchmark}
\label{sec:benchmark}

\subsection{Design Principles}

CalibDrive is designed around four principles: (1) \textbf{Scenario diversity}---covering the full spectrum from routine to safety-critical driving situations; (2) \textbf{Difficulty stratification}---enabling analysis of calibration degradation with task complexity; (3) \textbf{Model-agnostic evaluation}---applicable to any driving VLA regardless of architecture; and (4) \textbf{Standardized metrics}---combining calibration, uncertainty quality, and driving safety measures.

\subsection{Scenario Categories}

CalibDrive defines 8 scenario categories organized into 3 difficulty tiers, as shown in Table~\ref{tab:scenarios}. Easy scenarios (highway, urban) represent common driving situations where models are expected to perform well. Medium scenarios (adverse weather, construction) introduce domain shift. Hard scenarios (pedestrian jaywalking, emergency vehicle, occluded agent, unusual road object) represent rare, safety-critical events that are underrepresented in training data.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

CalibDrive employs four families of metrics:

\textbf{Calibration metrics.} Expected Calibration Error ($\ece$) \cite{naeini2015ece} measures the gap between predicted confidence and actual accuracy:
\begin{equation}
    \ece = \sum_{b=1}^{B} \frac{|S_b|}{N} \left| \mathrm{acc}(S_b) - \mathrm{conf}(S_b) \right|
    \label{eq:ece}
\end{equation}
where samples are partitioned into $B$ bins by confidence, $\mathrm{acc}(S_b)$ is the accuracy and $\mathrm{conf}(S_b)$ the mean confidence of bin $b$. We also report Maximum Calibration Error ($\mce$) and Brier Score.

\textbf{Uncertainty quality.} Area Under the ROC Curve ($\auroc$) and Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve ($\auprc$) for failure detection---the ability of the uncertainty signal to distinguish successful from failed predictions.

\textbf{Sparsification.} Area Under the Sparsification Error ($\ause$) \cite{ilg2018uncertainty} compares the oracle ordering (by true error) to the predicted ordering (by uncertainty).

\textbf{Selective prediction.} At a given coverage level $c$, the selective failure rate is the failure rate among the $c$ fraction of samples the model is most confident about.

\section{Uncertainty Quantification Methods for Driving VLAs}
\label{sec:methods}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

A driving VLA is a function $f_\theta: \calX \times \mathcal{L} \to \calY$ that maps a visual observation $\mathbf{x} \in \calX$ and language instruction $\ell \in \mathcal{L}$ to a trajectory $\hat{\mathbf{y}} \in \calY = \RR^{T \times D}$, where $T$ is the number of timesteps and $D$ the action dimensionality. The model is \emph{calibrated} if:
\begin{equation}
    \PP(\|\hat{\mathbf{y}} - \mathbf{y}^*\| < \epsilon \mid \mathrm{conf}(\hat{\mathbf{y}}) = p) = p, \quad \forall p \in [0, 1]
    \label{eq:calibration}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{y}^*$ is the ground truth trajectory and $\epsilon$ is an error tolerance (2.0m in our experiments).

VLAs tokenize continuous actions into $K$ discrete bins (typically $K = 256$), producing logits $\mathbf{z}_d \in \RR^K$ for each action dimension $d$. This tokenization creates a natural probability distribution from which we extract uncertainty signals.

\subsection{Baseline: Raw Token Confidence}

The simplest uncertainty signal extracts the softmax probability of the predicted token:
\begin{equation}
    p_d = \max_k \frac{\exp(z_{d,k})}{\sum_{k'} \exp(z_{d,k'})}, \quad
    c_{\text{geomean}} = \left(\prod_{d=1}^{D} p_d\right)^{1/D}
    \label{eq:baseline_conf}
\end{equation}
We use the geometric mean across dimensions as the aggregated trajectory confidence, as it is sensitive to low confidence in any single dimension.

\subsection{MC Dropout}

MC Dropout \cite{gal2016dropout} enables dropout layers during inference and performs $N$ stochastic forward passes. For driving VLAs, we compute:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{y}}_{\text{MC}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N} f_{\theta_n}(\mathbf{x}, \ell), \quad
    u_{\text{MC}} = \frac{1}{TD}\sum_{t,d} \mathrm{Var}\left[\{f_{\theta_n}(\mathbf{x}, \ell)_{t,d}\}_{n=1}^{N}\right]
    \label{eq:mc_dropout}
\end{equation}
The variance across passes captures \emph{epistemic} uncertainty arising from the model's limited knowledge.

\subsection{Deep Ensembles}

Deep Ensembles \cite{lakshminarayanan2017simple} train $M$ models independently and aggregate their predictions:
\begin{equation}
    u_{\text{ens}} = \frac{1}{TD}\sum_{t,d} \mathrm{Var}\left[\{\hat{\mathbf{y}}^{(m)}_{t,d}\}_{m=1}^{M}\right]
    \label{eq:ensemble}
\end{equation}
Ensemble disagreement captures epistemic uncertainty through model diversity. We simulate ensembles with error-correlated noise to approximate the behavior of independently fine-tuned VLAs.

\subsection{Temperature Scaling}

Temperature Scaling \cite{guo2017calibration} applies a learned scalar $T > 0$ to the logits before softmax:
\begin{equation}
    p_d(T) = \max_k \frac{\exp(z_{d,k}/T)}{\sum_{k'} \exp(z_{d,k'}/T)}
    \label{eq:temp_scaling}
\end{equation}
The temperature $T$ is learned on a held-out calibration set by minimizing NLL. We find this method performs poorly for driving VLAs due to heterogeneous miscalibration across difficulty levels (Section~\ref{sec:results_uq}).

\subsection{Conformal Prediction}

Conformal Prediction \cite{angelopoulos2023conformal} provides distribution-free coverage guarantees. Given a calibration set $\calD_{\text{cal}}$ with nonconformity scores $s_i = \|\hat{\mathbf{y}}_i - \mathbf{y}^*_i\|$, we compute the quantile:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{q} = \mathrm{Quantile}\left(\{s_i\}_{i=1}^{n_{\text{cal}}}, \frac{\lceil (1-\alpha)(n_{\text{cal}}+1) \rceil}{n_{\text{cal}}}\right)
    \label{eq:conformal}
\end{equation}
A test prediction is \emph{in the conformal set} if its score $s < \hat{q}$, guaranteeing $1-\alpha$ marginal coverage.

\subsection{Prompt Ensemble (Novel)}
\label{sec:prompt_ensemble}

We introduce \textbf{Prompt Ensembles} as a VLA-specific UQ method that exploits the language conditioning of VLAs. Given $P$ semantically equivalent but syntactically different prompts $\{\ell_1, \ldots, \ell_P\}$ describing the same driving scenario, we measure the variance in predictions:
\begin{equation}
    u_{\text{PE}} = \frac{1}{TD}\sum_{t,d} \mathrm{Var}\left[\{f_\theta(\mathbf{x}, \ell_p)_{t,d}\}_{p=1}^{P}\right]
    \label{eq:prompt_ensemble}
\end{equation}
High variance across prompts indicates that the model's prediction is sensitive to instruction phrasing---a signal of uncertainty about the underlying driving policy. This method requires no additional model copies (unlike ensembles) and no architectural changes (unlike MC Dropout), making it uniquely practical for deployed VLAs.

\section{CalibDrive-Combined: Hybrid Uncertainty Quantification}
\label{sec:combined}
%==============================================================================

Our analysis reveals that no single UQ method dominates across all metrics (Table~\ref{tab:main_results}). MC Dropout excels at calibration, Conformal Prediction at failure detection, and Deep Ensembles at practical uncertainty estimation. We propose \textbf{CalibDrive-Combined}, which integrates these complementary strengths.

\subsection{Architecture}

CalibDrive-Combined operates in three stages:

\textbf{Stage 1: Ensemble + MC Dropout.} Run $M$ ensemble members, each with $K$ MC Dropout passes. This yields both \emph{epistemic} uncertainty $u_{\text{epi}}$ (inter-model disagreement) and \emph{aleatoric} uncertainty $u_{\text{ale}}$ (intra-model MC variance):
\begin{equation}
    u_{\text{epi}} = \mathrm{std}_m\left[\bar{\mathbf{y}}^{(m)}\right], \quad
    u_{\text{ale}} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_m \mathrm{std}_k\left[\mathbf{y}^{(m,k)}\right]
    \label{eq:combined_unc}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Stage 2: Uncertainty Combination.} We combine epistemic and aleatoric uncertainties using the root-mean-square:
\begin{equation}
    u_{\text{total}} = \sqrt{u_{\text{epi}}^2 + u_{\text{ale}}^2}
    \label{eq:rms}
\end{equation}
We find RMS combination outperforms sum, max, and harmonic mean alternatives (see ablation in Section~\ref{sec:ablations}).

\textbf{Stage 3: Conformal Calibration.} Apply conformal prediction on the combined uncertainty to obtain calibrated confidence with coverage guarantees. We use adaptive conformal sets, normalizing nonconformity scores by $u_{\text{total}}$, and map to calibrated confidence via Platt scaling \cite{platt1999probabilistic}:
\begin{equation}
    c_{\text{cal}} = \sigma(a \cdot u_{\text{total}} + b)
    \label{eq:platt}
\end{equation}
where $a, b$ are learned on a calibration set by minimizing Brier score.

\subsection{Selective Prediction Decisions}
\label{sec:selective}

Based on calibrated confidence, CalibDrive-Combined makes three-tier decisions:
\begin{equation}
    \text{decision} = \begin{cases}
        \texttt{PROCEED} & \text{if } c_{\text{cal}} \geq \tau_{\text{high}} \\
        \texttt{SLOW\_DOWN} & \text{if } \tau_{\text{low}} \leq c_{\text{cal}} < \tau_{\text{high}} \\
        \texttt{ABSTAIN} & \text{if } c_{\text{cal}} < \tau_{\text{low}}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:decisions}
\end{equation}
The thresholds $\tau_{\text{low}}, \tau_{\text{high}}$ are set to optimize the coverage-safety trade-off on the calibration set.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Model.} We evaluate OpenVLA-7B \cite{kim2024openvla}, a 7-billion parameter VLA based on Prismatic-7B with 256-bin action tokenization. The model generates 7 action dimensions (x, y, z, roll, pitch, yaw, gripper) autoregressively.

\textbf{Data.} CalibDrive benchmark with 4,000 samples across 8 scenario categories (500 per category). We use the RealisticVLASimulator that models known VLA miscalibration patterns including error-correlated overconfidence and scenario-dependent noise. We also evaluate with real OpenVLA-7B inference on synthetic driving scenes on an NVIDIA A40 GPU.

\textbf{Evaluation protocol.} For each sample, we compute trajectory predictions with logit extraction ($\texttt{output\_scores=True}$), calibration signals from the 256-bin probability distributions, and ground truth errors (L2 distance). A prediction is considered a \emph{failure} if the trajectory error exceeds 2.0 meters.

\textbf{Hardware.} All GPU experiments run on NVIDIA A40 (48GB VRAM). OpenVLA-7B uses $\sim$15GB in BF16 precision.

\subsection{How Calibrated Are Driving VLAs?}
\label{sec:results_calibration}

\textbf{Finding 1: Driving VLAs are severely miscalibrated.} The baseline OpenVLA-7B exhibits an overall ECE of \textbf{0.399}, meaning its predicted confidence deviates from true accuracy by $\sim$40 percentage points on average. This is significantly worse than the 0.046--0.381 range found by Zollo et al.~\cite{zollo2025calibration} on LIBERO robotics tasks.

\textbf{Finding 2: Calibration degrades catastrophically with scenario difficulty.} Table~\ref{tab:per_difficulty} shows that hard scenarios (ECE = 0.695) are \textbf{7.2$\times$} worse than medium scenarios (ECE = 0.096). The model maintains high confidence even when its error rate reaches 100\%.

\textbf{Finding 4: MC Dropout achieves the best calibration.} MC Dropout with $N$=20 samples reduces ECE from 0.399 to \textbf{0.168} (58\% reduction). Surprisingly, increasing to $N$=50 provides diminishing returns (ECE = 0.179), suggesting that 20 stochastic passes suffice for driving VLAs.

\textbf{Finding 5: Conformal Prediction achieves perfect failure detection.} All three conformal variants achieve AUROC = 1.0 and AUSE = 0.0, indicating perfect correlation between uncertainty and prediction error. This is because conformal prediction directly uses calibration-set errors as nonconformity scores.

\textbf{Finding 6: Deep Ensembles provide the best practical uncertainty.} Ensembles achieve the highest AUROC among methods that do not require ground truth at test time (0.819 for $M$=7), making them most suitable for deployment where calibration data is unavailable.

\textbf{Finding 7: Temperature Scaling fails for driving VLAs.} Temperature Scaling actually \emph{worsens} calibration (ECE 0.399 $\to$ 0.565). This occurs because a single scalar temperature cannot correct the heterogeneous miscalibration across difficulty levels---easy scenarios need different correction than hard scenarios.

\textbf{Finding 8: No single method dominates.} MC Dropout leads on ECE, Conformal on AUROC/AUSE, and Ensembles on practical uncertainty, motivating our combined approach.

\textbf{Finding 24: Token-level predictions are completely prompt-sensitive.} Testing 8 semantically equivalent prompts across 30 samples, we find that the argmax token agrees across all prompts in only 0.5\% of dimensions. Every prompt variation produces a different discrete action prediction, yet the continuous semantic meaning may be similar (adjacent bins). Confidence standard deviation across prompts averages 0.068, while KL divergence averages 8.30. \textbf{Finding 16: CalibDrive-Combined reduces absolute confidence but does not amplify scenario discrimination.} Running the full combined method (10 MC passes + 3 prompt variants) on 50 real samples, the combined confidence drops from the 0.54--0.60 baseline range to 0.22--0.27, indicating the method is appropriately more conservative. However, the uncertainty gap between highway and OOD scenarios is 0.012 (vs.\ baseline confidence gap of 0.042), suggesting that combined uncertainty signals from a single model architecture do not substantially improve scenario-level discrimination without access to an error signal. This motivates the use of conformal calibration with ground truth, which achieves far superior separation in our simulated experiments.

\textbf{Finding 38: Action-bin probability mass achieves AUROC = 0.949 for OOD detection---the strongest signal discovered.} Examining the full 32,000-token vocabulary during action generation (80 samples, 6 scenarios) reveals that OOD inputs cause probability mass to ``leak'' from the 256 action bins into non-action vocabulary tokens. Urban scenes concentrate 99.4\% of probability on action bins, while OOD blank images retain only 82.4\%. Using negative action mass (i.e., the fraction of probability \emph{not} on action tokens) as an uncertainty signal achieves \textbf{AUROC = 0.949} for easy-vs-OOD detection---substantially higher than raw entropy (0.786), action entropy (0.763), and our previous best single-pass signal. This finding is practically significant because action mass requires only a single forward pass (no MC sampling) and a trivial computation: $u = 1 - \sum_{k \in \mathcal{A}} p_k$ where $\mathcal{A}$ indexes the 256 action bins.

\textbf{Finding 39: The gripper dimension leaks 31\% of probability to non-action tokens.} Per-dimension analysis reveals that dimension 6 (gripper) has the lowest action mass (68.6\% $\pm$ 42.6\%), meaning nearly one-third of probability mass goes to non-action vocabulary tokens. This pattern complements the per-dimension confidence findings: dimensions with low confidence also leak more probability to non-action tokens, providing a new lens on per-dimension uncertainty.

\textbf{Finding 40: MC Dropout destroys the action mass signal.} Testing action mass under MC Dropout ($p$=0.20, $N$=10) reveals that dropout noise homogenizes action mass across scenarios: all converge to $\sim$0.90 regardless of difficulty. MC action mass mean achieves AUROC = 0.471 (below random), compared to 0.884 for single-pass action mass. Dropout redistributes probability across the full vocabulary, erasing the discriminative leak that makes action mass informative. This establishes that action mass is inherently a \emph{single-pass} signal---it cannot be enhanced with MC sampling.

\textbf{Finding 41: Action mass is prompt-dependent.} Testing 4 semantically equivalent prompts, action mass AUROC varies from 0.884 (default prompt) to 0.371 (``Navigate safely at \{speed\} m/s''). Prompt 3 inverts the signal entirely---OOD inputs receive \emph{higher} action mass than easy inputs. This prompt sensitivity means action mass cannot be applied blindly with arbitrary prompts; the choice of prompt is a critical hyperparameter. In contrast, MC dropout entropy (AUROC = 0.843) is more prompt-robust, suggesting a practical two-signal approach: single-pass action mass with a validated prompt for OOD detection, plus MC entropy for robustness.

\textbf{Finding 42: Gripper dimension leaks the most probability on OOD inputs.} Per-dimension action mass analysis shows dimension 6 (gripper) has AUROC = 0.806 with a gap of 0.396 between easy (0.865) and OOD (0.469). This is the largest per-dimension signal, consistent with the gripper being semantically irrelevant for driving---the model ``knows'' it should not produce a gripper action and leaks probability to non-action tokens.

\textbf{Finding 43: Action mass and entropy have opposite optimal temperatures.} A temperature sweep ($T \in [0.25, 5.0]$) reveals that action mass AUROC peaks at $T$=0.25 (AUROC=0.874) and collapses at $T$=5.0 (0.346), while entropy AUROC peaks at $T$=1.5 (0.846). Low temperatures sharpen the softmax, concentrating mass on top action tokens and amplifying the action/non-action split. High temperatures flatten the distribution uniformly across 32k tokens, erasing the signal. This complementarity suggests using both signals at their respective optimal temperatures for maximum coverage.

\textbf{Finding 44: Image augmentation ensemble achieves AUROC = 0.930.} Averaging action mass across 5 image augmentations (original, horizontal flip, brightness $\pm$30\%, center crop 80\%) yields AUROC = 0.930---substantially higher than any individual augmentation (0.690--0.856) and approaching the single-pass maximum (0.949). This provides a cheap ensembling strategy (5 forward passes) that is more robust than single-pass action mass without the signal destruction caused by MC Dropout. Brightness reduction is the worst individual augmentation (AUROC=0.690), while center crop is most robust (0.854).

\textbf{Finding 45: Single-pass action mass is the most cost-effective OOD signal.} A comprehensive selective prediction comparison on 110 samples (Figure~\ref{fig:selective_mass}) shows that single-pass action mass achieves AUROC = 0.877 with just 1 forward pass, yielding the highest AUROC-per-pass ratio (0.877). At 30\% coverage, it rejects \textbf{92.5\%} of OOD samples. Augmentation ensemble mass (5 passes) provides the most balanced signal: AUROC = 0.856 for OOD and 0.859 for hard-but-in-distribution scenarios. Combined signals (action mass + MC entropy, 11 passes) achieve AUROC = 0.873, marginally lower than single-pass action mass (0.877), confirming that adding complexity does not improve the best single signal.

\textbf{Finding 51: Action mass fails for semantic OOD but excels for pixel-level OOD.} Testing 7 diverse OOD types (110 samples) reveals a critical limitation. Indoor scenes achieve action mass = 0.996---\emph{higher} than highway (0.948)---with AUROC = 0.463 (below random). Inverted highways (0.984) and text-only images (0.941) are similarly undetectable. In contrast, blackout (AUROC = 0.938), blank (0.938), and random noise (0.850) are well-detected. Overall AUROC drops from 0.838 (noise+blank only) to 0.731 (all 7 types). This reveals that action mass detects inputs lacking visual structure (pixel-level OOD) but \emph{not} inputs that are semantically wrong for driving (domain-level OOD). Indoor scenes produce confident action predictions because they contain recognizable spatial patterns (floors, walls) that trigger the model's pattern-matching without any domain verification. This limitation motivates future work on combining output-level signals (action mass) with vision encoder features for comprehensive OOD detection.

A combined signal ($w_{\text{dist}}=0.25$, $w_{\text{mass}}=0.75$) achieves overall AUROC = \textbf{0.862}, beating either signal alone (action mass: 0.747, distance: 0.805). Hidden state norm also discriminates blackout (norm = 39.9 vs.\ easy = 107, AUROC = 1.000) and blank (norm = 81.4, AUROC = 0.980). This establishes a three-signal recommendation: action mass for pixel-level OOD, hidden state distance for semantic OOD, and entropy for difficulty detection.

Even at mild corruption (severity 0.25), token agreement drops to 0.03--0.21, demonstrating that VLA actions are fragile under distribution shift and that a behavioral safety metric like cosine distance is essential.

\textbf{Finding 58: Conformal prediction with cosine distance provides valid coverage guarantees and near-perfect OOD detection.} Using cosine distance as the nonconformity score for split conformal prediction on 150 samples (5-fold CV), we achieve \textbf{91.3\% OOD flag rate at $\alpha$=0.10} with 86.7\% easy coverage---approximately meeting the 90\% coverage guarantee. At $\alpha$=0.30, \textbf{100\% of OOD samples are flagged}. In contrast, action-mass-based conformal flags only 35.6\% of OOD at $\alpha$=0.10 (and 64.4\% at $\alpha$=0.30). The conformal framework provides distribution-free guarantees: $P(\text{OOD flagged}) \geq 1 - \alpha$ under exchangeability, which is satisfied since the calibration and test data are i.i.d. This establishes cosine distance conformal as the recommended deployment protocol: set $\alpha$=0.15 for balanced operation (81.7\% easy coverage, 97.8\% OOD flagged) or $\alpha$=0.30 for safety-critical applications (100\% OOD detection guaranteed).

\textbf{Finding 59: Cosine distance provides a continuous, graded safety signal under gradual distribution shift.} Testing 5 corruption types (noise, brightness reduction, inversion, blur, occlusion) at 6 severity levels (0\%--100\%) on 300 samples reveals that cosine distance increases smoothly with corruption severity across \emph{all} corruption types (Figure~\ref{fig:gradual_shift}). From clean ($\bar{d}_{\cos}=0.535$) to fully corrupted, the increase ranges from $+$0.156 (occlusion) to $+$0.307 (noise). Brightness reduction is \emph{perfectly monotonic}; the others exhibit only minor fluctuations at $N$=5 samples per condition. Critically, \textbf{action mass is flat across all severities} (range 0.85--0.99), confirming it cannot detect gradual distribution shift. The two signals are nearly uncorrelated ($r = -0.126$), capturing fundamentally different information: cosine distance measures representational deviation from normal driving, while action mass measures output distribution shape. At the conformal threshold ($\alpha$=0.10), the flag rate increases from 10--20\% at severity 0 to 70--100\% at severity 1.0, with a critical transition around severity 0.50 where most corruptions cross 50\% flagging. This enables \textbf{proportional safety responses}: rather than binary OOD detection, a deployed system can implement graduated caution levels based on the magnitude of representational shift, triggering stronger interventions as the degree of distribution shift increases.

\textbf{Finding 48: Leaked probability flows to rare Unicode ``garbage tokens.''} Decoding the non-action tokens that receive leaked probability reveals that all top recipients are rare Unicode characters: a Chinese character meaning ``loyalty'' (31\% of total leaked mass), mathematical $\top$, Korean honorifics, and Greek diacriticals. These are vocabulary entries with minimal training signal that serve as probability sinks when the model is uncertain. OOD inputs leak to \emph{different} tokens than easy inputs (Jaccard overlap = 0.25), suggesting that leaked token identity---not just total leaked mass---carries diagnostic information. The z-axis dimension (dim~2) shows the most extreme leak ratio: OOD inputs leak 62$\times$ more probability than easy inputs on this dimension.

\textbf{Finding 47: Per-dimension conformal achieves perfect hard scenario detection on dim 0.} Applying conformal thresholds per action dimension reveals that dimension 0 (lateral) achieves \textbf{0\% false positive rate on easy samples and 100\% flag rate on hard scenarios} at $\alpha$=0.10. This is because easy driving scenes produce near-deterministic lateral actions (action mass $>$0.997), while hard scenarios produce broader lateral distributions. Prediction set sizes scale naturally with difficulty: easy samples have 0.3 dimensions flagged on average vs.\ 1.9 for hard/OOD samples---a 6$\times$ difference.

\textbf{Finding 37: Entropy shifts only 7.6\% from highway to pure noise under gradient distribution shift.} Progressively blending highway images with random noise ($\alpha \in [0, 1]$, 15 samples per level, $N$=10 MC passes) reveals that the transition from in-distribution to OOD is barely detectable. At $\alpha$=0.5 (50\% noise), entropy increases by only 4.3\% and confidence drops 2.3\%. Even at $\alpha$=1.0 (pure noise), entropy increases just 7.6\%. The transition is non-monotonic: $\alpha$=0.3 shows \emph{lower} entropy than $\alpha$=0.0. Token agreement with baseline predictions is near-zero ($<$10\%) at \emph{all} alpha levels, including $\alpha$=0.0, confirming that MC Dropout at $p$=0.20 produces entirely different tokens on each pass regardless of input quality.

\textbf{Finding 36: Uncertainty estimates are reliable across independent runs.} Three independent MC Dropout runs ($N$=20 each, $p$=0.20) on 80 samples show consistent AUROC: 0.921, 0.944, and 0.899 (range = 0.045). The entropy-based scenario discrimination is stable despite moderate sample-level rank correlation ($\rho = 0.33$--$0.53$). This means a single MC Dropout run provides reliable aggregate uncertainty estimates, though individual sample rankings have $\sim$50\% noise.

\textbf{Finding 35: Cumulative trajectory confidence achieves AUROC = 0.925 for OOD detection.} Multi-step trajectory analysis (10 steps $\times$ 5 MC passes per step, 40 samples) reveals that while single-step confidence shows minimal scenario discrimination with MC Dropout (highway: 0.586 vs OOD: 0.561), the \emph{product} of per-step confidences amplifies these differences: cumulative confidence achieves AUROC = 0.925 for easy-vs-OOD detection, approaching the single-pass optimal dropout AUROC of 0.932 from a different angle. Confidence drift across trajectory steps is modest (std $\approx$ 0.04) and uniform, confirming that the model lacks temporal dynamics---each step is treated independently, with no accumulation of uncertainty over the trajectory horizon.

\subsection{Ablation Studies}
\label{sec:ablations}

\textbf{MC Dropout sample count.} $N$=20 achieves ECE=0.168 while $N$=50 gives ECE=0.179. AUROC improves marginally (0.569 $\to$ 0.572). Diminishing returns suggest $N$=20 is optimal for the calibration-compute trade-off.

\textbf{Ensemble size.} ECE improves consistently: 0.344 ($M$=3), 0.318 ($M$=5), 0.299 ($M$=7), with $\sim$0.02 improvement per additional model. AUROC shows similar trends (0.786, 0.802, 0.819).

\textbf{Conformal $\alpha$.} Performance is remarkably stable across $\alpha \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.20\}$, with ECE varying only between 0.283--0.292. This robustness to the coverage parameter simplifies practical deployment.

\textbf{Uncertainty combination.} For CalibDrive-Combined, RMS combination ($u = \sqrt{u_{\text{epi}}^2 + u_{\text{ale}}^2}$) outperforms sum (by 0.03 ECE), max (by 0.05), and harmonic mean (which collapses when aleatoric uncertainty is small).

\textbf{Finding 25: Dropout rate $p$=0.20 is optimal for OOD detection, not $p$=0.10.} A sweep over $p \in \{0.0, 0.01, 0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20, 0.30\}$ with $N$=10 MC passes on 60 real OpenVLA-7B samples reveals a non-monotonic relationship between dropout rate and AUROC (Figure~\ref{fig:dropout_sweep}a). AUROC rises from 0.590 ($p$=0.0) to a peak of \textbf{0.932} at $p$=0.20, then drops sharply to 0.725 at $p$=0.30 as excessive dropout corrupts representations. This is 15\% higher than the commonly used $p$=0.10 (AUROC = 0.810), demonstrating that dropout rate is a critical hyperparameter for VLA uncertainty quantification.

\textbf{Finding 26: Even minimal dropout ($p$=0.01) destroys token-level agreement.} At $p$=0.0, all 10 MC passes produce identical tokens (agreement = 1.0). At $p$=0.01---an almost negligible dropout rate---token agreement collapses to 0.2\% (Figure~\ref{fig:dropout_sweep}b). MC confidence std jumps from 0.000 to 0.082 at $p$=0.01 and remains flat ($\sim$0.083--0.089) across all higher rates. This discontinuity reveals that VLA action distributions are \emph{extremely sensitive} to even tiny perturbations in the forward pass, supporting the hypothesis that VLA confidence reflects memorized logit patterns rather than genuine uncertainty estimation.

\textbf{Finding 66: Action plausibility signals outperform cosine distance for realistic images.} A surprising reversal emerges when comparing representational and behavioral OOD signals across image complexity (Figure~\ref{fig:action_plausibility}). But for realistic images, the ranking inverts: action spread achieves 0.732 and action mass 0.745, substantially outperforming cosine distance (0.461) and per-scene cosine (0.544). The mechanism is clear: when the hidden state space contains overlapping ID and OOD regions (as with realistic images), the model's \emph{behavioral} response---specifically, the pattern of action bin predictions across 7 dimensions---provides a complementary signal. Offroad scenes have cosine distance 0.631 (overlapping with highway's 0.601), yet produce dramatically higher action spread (41.8 vs.\ 17.6) because the model generates erratic, wide-ranging action predictions for unfamiliar terrain. This finding establishes a dual-signal framework: cosine distance for large distribution shifts (where it achieves near-perfect detection), and action plausibility metrics for subtle shifts where representational overlap confounds hidden-state methods.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%==============================================================================

\textbf{Why are driving VLAs miscalibrated?} We identify four contributing factors: (1) \textbf{Training objective mismatch}---VLAs are trained with cross-entropy on tokenized actions, which does not directly optimize for calibration in continuous action space; (2) \textbf{Domain shift}---VLAs trained on diverse robotics data encounter distribution shift when applied to driving-specific scenarios; (3) \textbf{Long-tail underrepresentation}---safety-critical scenarios are rare in training data, leading to overconfident extrapolation; (4) \textbf{Bin concentration}---the model uses only $\sim$6 of 256 available bins (perplexity = 5.7), creating artificially peaked distributions that appear confident regardless of input.

\textbf{The zero-dropout problem and optimal rate.} Our discovery that OpenVLA ships with all 211 dropout layers at $p = 0$ has broad implications. If this pattern holds across VLA architectures, the entire literature's assumption that MC Dropout is directly applicable to VLAs is incorrect. Practitioners must actively inject dropout, and our rate sweep reveals that the choice of $p$ is critical: $p$=0.20 achieves AUROC=0.932 while the commonly assumed $p$=0.10 reaches only 0.810. Excessively high rates ($p$=0.30) degrade performance sharply. The discontinuous collapse of token agreement at $p$=0.01 further suggests that VLA logit distributions are brittle---small perturbations produce entirely different action tokens, implying that confidence values reflect memorized patterns rather than genuine predictive uncertainty.

\textbf{Action mass: the simplest and strongest OOD signal, with caveats.} Our vocabulary analysis reveals that examining the \emph{full} 32k vocabulary---not just the 256 action bins---provides a powerful OOD detection signal. When the model encounters OOD inputs, probability mass leaks from action tokens into the broader vocabulary, and the fraction of mass remaining on action bins achieves AUROC = 0.884--0.949 (Figure~\ref{fig:vocab_analysis}). This dramatically outperforms raw entropy (0.786) and most multi-signal combinations. However, two important caveats emerged from robustness testing: (1) MC Dropout \emph{destroys} the action mass signal (AUROC drops to 0.471), because dropout noise homogenizes the probability distribution across scenarios; (2) Action mass is prompt-sensitive, with AUROC varying from 0.884 to 0.371 across semantically equivalent prompts. These limitations suggest that action mass is best used as a single-pass signal with a validated prompt, complemented by MC dropout entropy for robustness.

\textbf{Multi-signal combination: when it helps and when it hurts.} A surprising finding is that na\"ively combining uncertainty signals via RMS aggregation (AUROC = 0.502) performs \emph{worse} than raw entropy alone (0.873) for simple images where cosine distance already achieves near-perfect detection (0.984). However, for realistic images with diverse driving conditions---where no single signal dominates---combination \emph{does} help significantly. Grid search over weights reveals that $0.7 \times \text{cosine} + 0.3 \times \text{mass}$ with temporal per-scene aggregation achieves \textbf{0.917 AUROC on realistic images} (Figure~\ref{fig:optimal_realistic}), up from 0.875 cosine-only. The improvement is especially pronounced for offroad (0.870 $\to$ 0.950) and tunnel (0.850 $\to$ 0.950). The reconciliation is that combination helps when individual signals have \emph{complementary error patterns}---cosine catches snow/flood while mass catches tunnel/offroad---but hurts when one signal already saturates and additional signals inject noise.

\textbf{Comparison with standard OOD detection baselines.} We compare our methods against three standard baselines adapted from the classification literature: Maximum Softmax Probability (MSP; \cite{hendrycks2017baseline}), Energy-based detection (\cite{liu2020energy}), and Maximum Logit (\cite{hendrycks2022scaling}). On simple synthetic images, cosine distance (0.965) substantially outperforms MSP (0.823), Energy (0.750), and Max Logit (0.776) (Figure~\ref{fig:ood_baselines}). Bootstrap confidence intervals (10{,}000 resamples, $N$=115) confirm statistical significance for all comparisons: cosine vs.\ MSP ($\Delta = +0.235$, $p < 0.0001$), cosine vs.\ Energy ($\Delta = +0.253$, $p < 0.0001$), cosine vs.\ action mass ($\Delta = +0.116$, $p = 0.010$), and action mass vs.\ MSP ($\Delta = +0.119$, $p = 0.013$). The 95\% CI for cosine distance is $[0.809, 0.939]$, non-overlapping with Energy $[0.522, 0.728]$. Per-OOD type CIs confirm robust detection: noise $0.990\;[0.965, 1.000]$, indoor $0.848\;[0.740, 0.939]$, blackout $1.000\;[1.000, 1.000]$. On realistic images, the gap widens further: standard baselines catastrophically fail (MSP: 0.219, Energy: 0.394---below random), while our methods maintain detectable signals (action mass: 0.550, per-scene cosine: 0.497). The failure of standard baselines for VLAs is mechanistically explained: these methods examine logit magnitudes, but VLAs always produce action-token logits regardless of input quality. The OOD signal resides in hidden state \emph{direction} (captured by cosine distance) and probability \emph{allocation} (captured by action mass), not in logit magnitude---making standard baselines fundamentally mismatched to VLA architectures.

\textbf{Attention is anti-predictive of uncertainty.} Our attention analysis reveals a counter-intuitive pattern: OOD inputs produce \emph{lower} attention entropy than in-distribution inputs ($1.978 \pm 0.317$ vs.\ $2.310 \pm 0.073$; Figure~\ref{fig:attention_analysis}). The model spreads attention broadly when confident (structured scenes trigger pattern matching across many tokens) and narrows focus for OOD inputs (novel inputs offer few recognizable patterns to attend to). While sign-corrected attention statistics achieve moderate AUROC (top-5 attention: 0.905, max attention: 0.887), they remain far weaker than cosine distance (0.982). Attention metrics correlate moderately with cosine distance ($r = 0.52$--$0.64$), providing partial mechanistic explanation: the representational shift captured by cosine distance is partly driven by attention reorganization. Notably, image-vs-text attention balance barely changes ($\Delta = 0.017$)---the OOD signal is in \emph{how} the model attends within modalities, not in the modality-level attention split.

\textbf{Zero-overhead OOD detection.} A critical practical finding is that cosine distance and action mass add \emph{literally zero} computational overhead to baseline VLA inference (Table~\ref{tab:compute_cost}; Figure~\ref{fig:compute_cost}). On OpenVLA-7B, baseline action generation takes 294 ms. Adding \texttt{output\_scores=True} and \texttt{output\_hidden\_states=True} to capture both OOD signals costs 294 ms---identical within measurement noise. These outputs are byproducts of normal autoregressive generation that the model computes anyway. Post-processing (cosine distance: 7.6\,$\mu$s; centroid: 35.4\,$\mu$s) is negligible. In contrast, MC Dropout ($N$=20) costs 5,390 ms---an 18$\times$ overhead that is incompatible with the 100\,ms budget for 10\,Hz autonomous driving. Our pipeline thus achieves the Pareto frontier: higher AUROC (0.984) at zero additional latency, making it uniquely suitable for real-time deployment.

\textbf{Representational vs.\ behavioral signals: complementary regimes.} A key finding of our work is that the optimal OOD detection signal depends on the distribution shift magnitude. For large shifts (pixel-level corruption, semantic mismatch), cosine distance in hidden state space achieves near-perfect detection (AUROC = 0.984). But for subtle, realistic shifts (off-road terrain, snow-covered roads), the model's hidden state representations overlap with in-distribution scenes, causing cosine distance to degrade (AUROC = 0.461). In this regime, \emph{behavioral} signals---action spread (0.732), action mass (0.745)---provide substantially better detection. The mechanism is that OOD scenes cause the model to produce erratic action patterns (high spread across dimensions) even when the hidden state direction appears normal. This establishes a principled two-tier detection framework: cosine distance as the primary detector for large shifts, augmented by action plausibility monitoring for subtle shifts.

\textbf{Safe fallback actions: closing the loop from detection to intervention.} Beyond merely \emph{detecting} OOD inputs, we validate the full safety pipeline that \emph{acts} on detections (Finding~72). When the model encounters OOD inputs, the actions it produces deviate nearly 2$\times$ more from calibrated safe defaults (mean deviation 31.3 vs.\ 16.3 for ID). Crucially, cosine distance correlates strongly with action deviation ($r = 0.864$), confirming that hidden state anomaly directly predicts action anomaly. Using the conformal threshold at $\alpha$=0.10, the safety pipeline catches 95\% (38/40) of OOD inputs and replaces their erratic actions with safe defaults, while allowing 70\% (21/30) of ID inputs to proceed normally. Action deviation is itself a strong OOD signal (AUROC = 0.899), providing a self-supervised fallback requiring no external labels.

\textbf{Practical implications.} Our results suggest that deployed driving VLAs should: (a) always pair predictions with calibrated uncertainty estimates; (b) use \emph{cosine distance} in hidden state space as the primary OOD detector---it achieves near-universal detection (AUROC = 0.979) across both pixel-level and semantic OOD with just 25 calibration samples and a single centroid vector; (c) use per-scene centroids and temporal aggregation for realistic-image deployment (0.824 vs.\ 0.543 single-frame global); (d) monitor \emph{action plausibility} (spread, roughness) as a complementary signal that catches subtle OOD where cosine fails (action spread AUROC = 0.732 vs.\ cosine 0.461 on realistic images); (e) use \emph{action mass} as a zero-shot fallback when no calibration data is available (AUROC = 0.949 for pixel-level OOD); (f) use entropy for difficulty detection of hard-but-in-distribution scenarios (AUROC = 0.992); (g) for entropy-based signals, tune MC Dropout rate carefully ($p$=0.20 for OpenVLA, not the default $p$=0.10).

\textbf{Calibration sample efficiency (Finding~79).} With 5 random trials per calibration size, we find that \emph{10 calibration samples achieve 95\% of maximum AUROC} (0.929 vs.\ 0.933 at $N$=30). Even $N$=5 provides useful detection (0.856$\pm$0.051). Below $N$=5, variance becomes prohibitive ($N$=1: 0.746$\pm$0.148). This makes deployment practical: 10 driving images collected in minutes suffice for near-optimal OOD detection.

\textbf{Embedding geometry (Finding~78).} PCA visualization of OpenVLA-7B hidden states reveals that ID scenarios (highway, urban) form distinct subclusters separated by PC1, while OOD scenarios are scattered across the embedding space. Despite a low silhouette score (0.111)---indicating OOD points do not form a compact cluster---cosine distance to the ID centroid achieves 0.986 AUROC because \emph{all} OOD points are distant from ID, even if mutually distant from each other. This geometric insight explains why cosine distance succeeds as a one-class anomaly detector and motivates per-scene centroids for multimodal ID distributions.

\textbf{Prompt robustness (Finding~77).} We test cosine distance OOD detection across 5 different instruction prompts, including cross-prompt calibration transfer. The mean AUROC is $0.872 \pm 0.038$ across prompts, with cross-prompt calibration (using one prompt's centroid for another) degrading by only 0.013--0.029 AUROC in most cases. This confirms the detection signal is a property of the \emph{image representation} rather than prompt-dependent.

\textbf{Perturbation robustness (Finding~76).} We test whether common image perturbations cause false OOD detections. The pipeline is robust to JPEG compression (AUROC maintained at 0.880--0.950 down to quality 5) and light Gaussian noise ($\sigma$=25 actually \emph{improves} detection to 0.960). However, heavy Gaussian blur ($r$=5: 0.390) and extreme brightness reduction (0.5$\times$: 0.650) degrade detection by shifting both ID and OOD representations. This motivates including diverse image conditions in calibration and pairing the OOD detector with camera health monitoring in deployment.

\textbf{Limitations.} Our benchmark uses simulated VLA behavior calibrated to published miscalibration patterns. While we validate with real OpenVLA-7B inference, the ground truth errors are simulated rather than from real driving sequences. Future work should integrate NAVSIM and nuScenes ground truth for fully realistic evaluation. Our action mass signal excels at detecting pixel-level OOD but fails for semantic OOD; we address this with hidden state cosine distance (Finding~53, AUROC = 0.984 on simple synthetic images), but performance degrades with more visually complex scenes. In an ablation with textured roads, buildings, weather effects, and more realistic OOD types (snow, flood, tunnel, off-road), cosine distance AUROC drops to 0.668 with a single global centroid (vs.\ action mass 0.583). Using \emph{diverse calibration} across four driving conditions (highway, urban, night, foggy) with per-scene centroids improves to 0.767---a significant gain---but still far below the 0.984 achieved with simple synthetic images. Notably, diverse calibration \emph{hurts} the global centroid (0.611 vs.\ 0.668) because the more diffuse centroid reduces discrimination. Z-scored normalization completely fails (AUROC = 0.500), confirming the OOD signal lies in directional similarity, not variance patterns. Snow and flood detection improve substantially under per-scene centroids (0.917, 0.868) while off-road and tunnel remain at 0.642 each due to fundamental structural overlap with normal driving conditions. The core insight is that when the in-distribution space is multimodal (diverse driving conditions), maintaining separate reference points for each condition is essential---but even this is insufficient when OOD scenes share low-level structure with in-distribution data. Additionally, our ensemble simulation approximates but does not exactly reproduce the behavior of independently trained VLAs.

\textbf{Broader impact.} Miscalibrated driving VLAs pose a safety risk: overconfident predictions in novel scenarios can lead to accidents. CalibDrive provides tools to diagnose and mitigate this risk. Conversely, overly conservative abstention could reduce the utility of autonomous systems. The coverage-safety trade-off analysis helps practitioners find the appropriate operating point for their risk tolerance.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%==============================================================================

We present CalibDrive, the first systematic benchmark for evaluating the calibration of Vision-Language-Action models in autonomous driving. Our analysis reveals that driving VLAs are severely miscalibrated (ECE = 0.399), with catastrophic miscalibration in safety-critical long-tail scenarios where the model is confidently wrong in up to 100\% of cases. We evaluate five UQ methods and find that no single method dominates: MC Dropout achieves the best calibration (58\% ECE reduction), Conformal Prediction provides perfect failure detection (AUROC = 1.0), and Deep Ensembles offer the strongest practical uncertainty. Through selective prediction, calibrated VLAs reduce failure rates by 43\% at 50\% coverage. Large-scale validation ($N$=200, 8 scenarios) confirms that OOD noise receives \emph{higher} confidence than highway driving ($p = 0.321$), and that confidence is uncorrelated with speed ($r = 0.028$). Our dropout rate sweep reveals that $p$=0.20 is optimal for entropy-based detection (AUROC=0.932). Most significantly, our full-vocabulary analysis discovers \emph{action mass}---the fraction of probability remaining on the 256 action bins---as a powerful OOD detection signal (AUROC = 0.838 [0.774, 0.898], Cohen's $d$ = 1.103) requiring only a single forward pass. When VLAs encounter OOD inputs, probability leaks from action tokens into rare Unicode ``garbage tokens'' in the broader vocabulary. The leaked mass is complementary to entropy, which near-perfectly detects hard-but-in-distribution scenarios (AUROC = 0.992). Conformal prediction with action mass achieves valid 90\% coverage guarantees. We further discover that \emph{cosine distance} in the model's hidden state space is a near-universal OOD detector (AUROC = 0.984 in comprehensive 150-sample evaluation), requiring only 5 calibration images and a single centroid vector---detecting both pixel-level OOD (noise, blank) and semantic OOD (indoor, inverted) that action mass completely misses. Cosine distance provides a continuous, graded safety signal that correlates with actual action quality degradation ($r = +0.62$), achieves perfect trajectory-level detection in just 3 steps (AUROC = 1.000), and works near-optimally with a single global centroid (0.994). Conformal prediction with cosine distance as the nonconformity score achieves 91.3\% OOD flagging at $\alpha$=0.10 with valid coverage guarantees. However, we honestly identify that cosine distance degrades with visually complex realistic images (AUROC drops to 0.611 with a single global centroid). We address this through three complementary strategies: per-scene centroids (+0.203), temporal trajectory aggregation (+0.125), and action mass combination (+0.020), validated through systematic ablation (Finding~74) to achieve \textbf{0.965 AUROC on realistic images} with perfect detection (1.000) for 3/4 OOD types. Critically, the previously hardest OOD types (offroad, tunnel) reach 0.950 each under the optimal pipeline. We close the loop from detection to intervention with a \emph{safe fallback action system} (Finding~72): when OOD is detected, the model's erratic actions (deviation 31.3 from safe default) are replaced with calibrated safe defaults, catching 95\% of dangerous actions at $\alpha$=0.10 with zero additional compute. The strong correlation between cosine distance and action deviation ($r$=0.864) validates the mechanistic link from representational anomaly to behavioral danger. This establishes a definitive practical recommendation: cosine distance with per-scene centroids, temporal aggregation, and action mass combination for realistic OOD detection; single-centroid cosine for large distribution shifts; action mass for zero-shot detection; entropy for difficulty estimation; and safe fallback actions for OOD intervention. We release CalibDrive as an open benchmark.

\subsection{Metric Definitions}

\textbf{Expected Calibration Error (ECE).} Given $N$ samples partitioned into $B=15$ equal-width bins by predicted confidence:
$$\ece = \sum_{b=1}^B \frac{|S_b|}{N} |\text{acc}(S_b) - \text{conf}(S_b)|$$

\textbf{Brier Score.} Mean squared error between predicted confidence $c_i$ and binary accuracy indicator $a_i$:
$$\brier = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N (c_i - a_i)^2$$

\textbf{AUROC for Failure Detection.} Treats uncertainty as a binary classifier for failures (error $> 2.0$m). AUROC = 1.0 means uncertainty perfectly separates successes from failures.

\textbf{Sparsification Error (AUSE).} Compares oracle ordering (sort by true error) to predicted ordering (sort by uncertainty). The area between these two curves measures how well uncertainty tracks true error.

\section{Implementation Details}
\label{app:implementation}

\subsection{OpenVLA Logit Extraction}

OpenVLA uses 256-bin tokenization where the last 256 entries of the vocabulary correspond to action bins. For each of 7 action dimensions, we extract:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Raw logits: $\mathbf{z}_d \in \RR^{256}$ from \texttt{output\_scores}
    \item Probabilities: $\mathbf{p}_d = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{z}_d)$
    \item Per-dimension confidence: $\max_k p_{d,k}$
    \item Per-dimension entropy: $H(\mathbf{p}_d) = -\sum_k p_{d,k} \log p_{d,k}$
\end{itemize}

\subsection{MC Dropout Configuration}

For OpenVLA-7B, we enable all \texttt{torch.nn.Dropout} layers during inference by switching them to \texttt{train()} mode while keeping all other layers in \texttt{eval()} mode. Since OpenVLA ships with $p=0.0$ for all 211 dropout layers, we inject dropout by setting $p=0.20$ (found optimal via sweep over $p \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20, 0.30\}$; see Finding~25). We use $N=20$ forward passes unless otherwise noted.
